Probabilistic Forecasting and Calibration

Published

15 August 2024

In this post, I provide an overview of a theory of probabilistic forecasting. While many of the foundational ideas in this area date back to the mid-twentieth century, the general formulation of this theory has undergone most of its development in the past couple decades and is still an active area of research.
The underlying idea here is that, in order to quantify uncertainties, predictions of unknown quantities ought to take the form of probability distributions. This immediately leads to many challenging questions; perhaps the most obvious being how do you evaluate the quality of a probabilistic forecast? If I predict that there is a 90% chance it will rain tomorrow but it doesn’t rain, did I do a bad job? Or was my probabilistic forecast reasonable but the realized outcome just happened to fall in that 10% “no rain” tail? As we will see, trying to answer questions like these by intuition alone can lead to sub-optimal strategies and counterintuitive results. A mathematical theory of forecasting provides the foundation to rigorously study such questions.

We begin this post by defining the theoretical framework that will be used to study questions related to probabilistic forecasting. We will then discuss the notion of what it means for a forecast to be “calibrated”, before detailing methods of forecast evaluation using so-called scoring rules. Those in the machine learning community are probably most familiar with the idea of probabilistic calibration through its application to problems of supervised classification (calibration curves, relaibility diagrams, etc.). We will discuss how these ideas fall out as a special case of the more general theory presented here.

A long list of references is provided at the end of this post. Tillman Gneiting has been instrumental in developing the modern theory of probabilistic forecasting, and his papers cited below are one of the major sources for the information summarized here. I also recommend Ryan Tibshirani’s lecture notes on scoring and calibration for a nice accessible overview.

Theoretical Framework

The Prediction Space

In general, probabilistic forecasts are issued in order to predict some unknown quantity. We will model this target quantity as a random variable YYY, taking values in some space Y\mathcal{Y}Y, and distributed according to some probability measure ν\nuν. Throughout we will write L(Y)=ν\mathcal{L}(Y) = \nuL(Y)=ν to mean that the random variable YYY has distribution (i.e., law) ν\nuν. In typical settings, the distribution ν\nuν is unknown to the forecaster, who only observes a realization y∈Yy \in \mathcal{Y}y∈Y of the random variable YYY. Prior to this observation, we assume the forecaster issues a probabilistic forecast μ\muμ in seeking to predict YYY, where μ\muμ is some probability measure encoding the forecaster’s prediction. In providing a theoretical model for real-world forecasting problems, notice that the two main quantities of interest here are the forecast μ\muμ and the observation YYY: the forecaster issues a forecast and then observes the realization.

While modeling YYY as a random quantity is a straightforward choice, we will also make the (perhaps less intuitive) decision to treat the forecast μ\muμ itself as a random element. That is, we model the forecast as a random (probability) measure. Think about how forecasts might be obtained in practice, via a combination of data, models, assumptions, expert options, etc. It therefore seems reasonable to model the process by which all of this information is transformed into a forecast as random. In a parallel universe where things unfolded slightly differently, a different forecast might be produced. Murphy and Winkler (1987) take this insight a step further, proposing that probabilistic forecasts ought to be studied on the basis of the joint distribution over (μ,Y)(\mu, Y)(μ,Y). It is reasonable to think that the processes by which the observation yyy is generated might also influence the generation of the forecast μ\muμ. For example, some meteorological processes that lead to a temperature observation YYY might contribute to other sources of observed data on which the forecast μ\muμ is based. The main takeaway here is that a theory of probabilistic forecasting should be based around the joint distribution between the forecast and the observation. We will call this joint probability space the prediction space. The below definition summarizes this idea, and fills in some of the measure-theoretical details.

Definition (Prediction Space). Let (Y,B)(\mathcal{Y}, \mathcal{B})(Y,B) be some measurable space of possible outcomes, and M\mathcal{M}M a space of probability measures over (Y,B)(\mathcal{Y}, \mathcal{B})(Y,B). A prediction space is a probability space (Ω,A,Q)(\Omega, \mathcal{A}, \mathbb{Q})(Ω,A,Q), along with a sub-σ\sigmaσ-algebra A1⊆A\mathcal{A}_1 \subseteq \mathcal{A}A1​⊆A, where Ω=M×Y\Omega = \mathcal{M} \times \mathcal{Y}Ω=M×Y.

The sub-σ\sigmaσ-algebra A1\mathcal{A}_1A1​ can be interpreted as the knowledge, or information basis, available to the forecaster. The condition A1⊆A\mathcal{A}_1 \subseteq \mathcal{A}A1​⊆A thus encodes the natural assumption that “nature” has a larger information basis than the forecaster. While the above definition focuses on defining a suitable measure space, it will typically be convenient to work on the level of random variables (elements). Note that we reserve the phrase random variable for random quantities assuming a finite number of values. Since, in general, μ\muμ lives in an infinite-dimensional space of measures we use the term random element or random quantity.

Prediction Spaces and Random Elements. Given a prediction space (Ω,A,Q)(\Omega, \mathcal{A}, \mathbb{Q})(Ω,A,Q), as above, we will write (μ,Y)(\mu, Y)(μ,Y) to denote a random element taking values in the measurable space (Ω,A)(\Omega, \mathcal{A})(Ω,A), and distributed according to Q\mathbb{Q}Q. Formally, this random element is defined with respect to some implicit underlying probability space (Ω′,B′,P′)(\Omega^\prime, \mathcal{B}^\prime, \mathbb{P}^\prime)(Ω′,B′,P′) such that (μ,Y)(⋅):Ω′→Ω(\mu, Y)(\cdot): \Omega^\prime \to \Omega(μ,Y)(⋅):Ω′→Ω. The measure Q\mathbb{Q}Q is thus the distribution of the random element (μ,Y)(\mu, Y)(μ,Y) given by the push-forward Q=P′∘(μ,Y)−1\mathbb{Q} = \mathbb{P}^\prime \circ (\mu, Y)^{-1}Q=P′∘(μ,Y)−1.

The above definition of the random element interprets random outcomes (μ,Y)(\mu, Y)(μ,Y) from the prediction space as a random element that inherits its randomness from some underlying probability space. To be clear on notation, L(μ,Y)=Q\mathcal{L}(\mu, Y) = \mathbb{Q}L(μ,Y)=Q and (μ,Y)∼Q(\mu, Y) \sim \mathbb{Q}(μ,Y)∼Q are two equivalent ways to express that the random element (μ,Y)(\mu, Y)(μ,Y) is distributed according to Q\mathbb{Q}Q. As mentioned above, we interpret A1\mathcal{A}_1A1​ as the knowledge available to the forecaster; hence, we should require that the random measure μ\muμ be measurable with respect to A1\mathcal{A}_1A1​. It need not be measurable with respect to nature’s knowledge A\mathcal{A}A, as the forecaster’s knowledge base may be more limited.

Assumption. The random element μ\muμ is measurable with respect to the sub-σ\sigmaσ-algebra A1\mathcal{A}_1A1​; that is, μ−1(M)∈B′, for all M∈A1. \mu^{-1}(M) \in \mathcal{B}^\prime, \text{ for all } M \in \mathcal{A}_1. μ−1(M)∈B′, for all M∈A1​. Note the slight abuse of notation in writing B′\mathcal{B}^\primeB′ and A1\mathcal{A}_1A1​ even though we are restricting to the measure portion of the joint space over (μ,Y)(\mu, Y)(μ,Y).

The notation here can get a bit thorny. We have formalized μ\muμ as a random element: a measurable map from Ω′\Omega^\primeΩ′ to M\mathcal{M}M. This means that the image μ(ω′)∈M\mu(\omega^\prime) \in \mathcal{M}μ(ω′)∈M is a probability measure for each ω′∈Ω′\omega^\prime \in \Omega^\primeω′∈Ω′; i.e., μ(ω′)(⋅):B→[0,1]. \mu(\omega^\prime)(\cdot): \mathcal{B} \to [0, 1]. μ(ω′)(⋅):B→[0,1]. The two arguments on the lefthand side are notationally a bit of a pain, so we will proceed by treating μ\muμ as a measure representing the image of the above measurable map, meaning that the ω′\omega^\primeω′ will be suppressed. We will thus write μ(B)\mu(B)μ(B) for B∈BB \in \mathcal{B}B∈B to mean the random measure BBB evaluated at the set B⊂YB \subset \mathcal{Y}B⊂Y.

Special Case: Real-Valued Quantities

The majority of forecasting literature has focused on the setting Y=R\mathcal{Y} = \mathbb{R}Y=R; i.e., the quantity of interest YYY assumes scalar values in the real line. Generalization to multivariate settings is an active research area and beyond the scope of this post. We now restrict the previous definition to this special case.

Definition (Prediction Space in the Scalar Setting). Let M\mathcal{M}M denote a space of probability measures over (R,B)(\mathbb{R}, \mathcal{B})(R,B), where B\mathcal{B}B is the Borel σ\sigmaσ-algebra on the real line. In this setting, a prediction space is a probability space (Ω,A,Q)(\Omega, \mathcal{A}, \mathbb{Q})(Ω,A,Q), where Ω=M×R\Omega = \mathcal{M} \times \mathbb{R}Ω=M×R, along with a sub-σ\sigmaσ-algebra A1\mathcal{A}_1A1​. As before, we will consider Q\mathbb{Q}Q as the law of random element (μ,Y)∈Ω(\mu, Y) \in \Omega(μ,Y)∈Ω. We will also identify the measures μ∈M\mu \in \mathcal{M}μ∈M with their cumulative distribution functions (CDFs) F(y):=μ([−∞,y])F(y) := \mu([-\infty, y])F(y):=μ([−∞,y]) and slightly abuse notation by writing F∈MF \in \mathcal{M}F∈M.
