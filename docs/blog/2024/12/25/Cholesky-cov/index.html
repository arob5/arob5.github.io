Cholesky Decomposition of a Covariance Matrix

Regression interpretation of the Cholesky decomposition, with applications to covariance estimation.

Published

25 December 2024

In this post we consider the Cholesky decomposition of the covariance matrix of a Gaussian distribution. The eigendecomposition of covariance matrices gives rise to the well-known method of principal components analysis. The Cholesky decomposition is not as widely discussed in this context, but also has a variety of useful statistical applications.

Setup and Background

The Cholesky Decomposition

The Cholesky decomposition of a positive definite matrix CCC is the unique factorization of the form C=LL⊤,(1) C = LL^\top, \tag{1} C=LL⊤,(1) where LLL is a lower-triangular matrix with positive diagonal elements (note that constraining the diagonal to be positive is required for uniqueness). A positive definite matrix can also be uniquely decomposed as C=LDL⊤,(2) C = LDL^\top, \tag{2} C=LDL⊤,(2) where LLL is lower-triangular with ones on the diagonal, and DDD is a diagonal matrix with positive entries on the diagonal. We will refer to this as the modified Cholesky decomposition, but it is also often called the LDL decomposition. Given the modified Cholesky decomposition C=L~DL~⊤C = \tilde{L}D\tilde{L}^\topC=L~DL~⊤, we can form (1) by setting L:=L~D1/2L := \tilde{L}D^{1/2}L:=L~D1/2. We refer to both L~\tilde{L}L~ and LLL as the lower Cholesky factor of CCC; which we are referring to will be clear from context. LLL is guaranteed to be invertible, and L−1L^{-1}L−1 is itself a lower-triangular matrix. Finally, note that we could also consider decompositions of the form C=UDU⊤,(3) C = UDU^\top, \tag{3} C=UDU⊤,(3) where UUU is upper triangular. This “reversed” Cholesky decomposition is not as common, but will show up at one point in this post.

Statistical Setup

Throughout this post we consider a random vector x:=(x(1),…,x(p))⊤∈Rp,(4) x := \left(x^{(1)}, \dots, x^{(p)} \right)^\top \in \mathbb{R}^p, \tag{4} x:=(x(1),…,x(p))⊤∈Rp,(4) with positive definite covariance C:=Cov[x]C := \text{Cov}[x]C:=Cov[x]. We will often assume that xxx is Gaussian, but this assumption is not required for some of the results discussed below. We focus on the (modified) Cholesky decomposition C=LDL⊤C = LDL^\topC=LDL⊤, letting L={ℓij}L = \{\ell_{ij}\}L={ℓij​} denote the entries of the lower Cholesky factor. For the modified decomposition, and D:=diag(d1,…,dp)D := \text{diag}(d_1, \dots, d_p)D:=diag(d1​,…,dp​), where di>0d_i > 0di​>0.

Let C=LDL⊤C = LDL^\topC=LDL⊤ and define the random variable ϵ:=L−1x,(5) \epsilon := L^{-1}x, \tag{5} ϵ:=L−1x,(5) which satisfies Cov[ϵ]=L−1CL−⊤=L−1LDL⊤L−⊤=D.(6) \text{Cov}[\epsilon] = L^{-1}CL^{-\top} = L^{-1}LDL^\top L^{-\top} = D. \tag{6} Cov[ϵ]=L−1CL−⊤=L−1LDL⊤L−⊤=D.(6) Thus, the map x↦L−1xx \mapsto L^{-1}xx↦L−1x outputs a “decorrelated” random vector. The inverse map ϵ↦Lϵ\epsilon \mapsto L\epsilonϵ↦Lϵ “re-correlates” ϵ\epsilonϵ, producing a random vector with covariance CCC. If we add on the assumption that xxx is Gaussian, then ϵ\epsilonϵ is a Gaussian vector with independent entries. The transformation LϵL\epsilonLϵ is the typical method used in simulating draws from a correlated Gaussian vector. Note that if we instead considered the standard Cholesky factorization C=LL⊤C = LL^\topC=LL⊤ with ϵ\epsilonϵ still defined as in (5), then Cov[ϵ]=I\text{Cov}[\epsilon] = ICov[ϵ]=I.

Conditional Variances and Covariances

We start by demonstrating how the (modified) Cholesky decomposition encodes information related to conditional variances and covariances between the x(j)x^{(j)}x(j). The below result considers conditional variances, and provides an interpretation of the diagonal entries of DDD in the Gaussian setting.

Proposition (conditional variances).
Let x∼N(m,C)x \sim \mathcal{N}(m,C)x∼N(m,C), with C=Cov[x]C = \text{Cov}[x]C=Cov[x] positive definite. Set ϵ:=L−1x\epsilon := L^{-1}xϵ:=L−1x, where C=LDL⊤C = LDL^\topC=LDL⊤. Then Var[x(j)∣x(1),…,x(j−1)]=dj,j=1,…,p(7) \text{Var}[x^{(j)}|x^{(1)}, \dots, x^{(j-1)}] = d_j, \qquad j = 1, \dots, p \tag{7} Var[x(j)∣x(1),…,x(j−1)]=dj​,j=1,…,p(7) where the j=1j=1j=1 case is interpreted as the unconditional variance Var[x(1)]\text{Var}[x^{(1)}]Var[x(1)]. If we instead define LLL by C=LL⊤C = LL^\topC=LL⊤, then Var[x(j)∣x(1),…,x(j−1)]=ℓjj2,j=1,…,p.(8) \text{Var}[x^{(j)}|x^{(1)}, \dots, x^{(j-1)}] = \ell^2_{jj}, \qquad j = 1, \dots, p. \tag{8} Var[x(j)∣x(1),…,x(j−1)]=ℓjj2​,j=1,…,p.(8)

Proof. From the definition x=Lϵx = L\epsilonx=Lϵ and the fact that LLL is lower triangular, we have x(j)=∑k=1jℓjkϵ(k).(9) x^{(j)} = \sum_{k=1}^{j} \ell_{jk} \epsilon^{(k)}. \tag{9} x(j)=k=1∑j​ℓjk​ϵ(k).(9) Thus,

\begin{align} \text{Var}[x^{(j)}|x^{(1)}, \dots, x^{(j-1)}] = \text{Var}\left[\sum_{k=1}^{j} \ell_{jk} \epsilon^{(k)} \bigg|x^{(1)}, \dots, x^{(j-1)}\right] &= \text{Var}\left[\sum_{k=1}^{j} \ell_{jk} \epsilon^{(k)} \bigg|\epsilon^{(1)}, \dots, \epsilon^{(j-1)}\right] \newline &= \text{Var}\left[\ell_{jj} \epsilon^{(j)}|\epsilon^{(1)}, \dots, \epsilon^{(j-1)}\right] \newline &= \text{Var}\left[\ell_{jj} \epsilon^{(j)}\right] \newline &= \ell^2_{jj} \text{Var}[\epsilon^{(j)}]. \tag{10} \end{align}

The first equality follows from the fact that xxx is an invertible transformation of ϵ\epsilonϵ, while the fourth uses the fact that the ϵ(j)\epsilon^{(j)}ϵ(j) are independent (owing to the Gaussian assumption). In the modified Cholesky case, (10) simplifies to ℓjj2Var[ϵ(j)]=1×dj=dj\ell^2_{jj} \text{Var}[\epsilon^{(j)}] = 1 \times d_j = d_jℓjj2​Var[ϵ(j)]=1×dj​=dj​. For standard Cholesky, it becomes ℓjj2Var[ϵ(j)]=ℓjj2⋅1=ℓjj2\ell^2_{jj} \text{Var}[\epsilon^{(j)}] = \ell^2_{jj} \cdot 1 = \ell^2_{jj}ℓjj2​Var[ϵ(j)]=ℓjj2​⋅1=ℓjj2​. ■\qquad \blacksquare■

Thus, the diagonal entries of DDD give the variances of the x(j)x^{(j)}x(j), conditional on all preceding entries in the vector. Clearly, the interpretation depends on the ordering of the entries, a fact that will be true for many results that rely on the Cholesky decomposition.

We can generalize the above result to consider conditional covariances instead of variances, which yields an interpretation of the off-diagonal elements of LLL.

Proposition (conditional covariances).
Let x∼N(m,C)x \sim \mathcal{N}(m,C)x∼N(m,C), with C=Cov[x]C = \text{Cov}[x]C=Cov[x] positive definite. Set ϵ:=L−1x\epsilon := L^{-1}xϵ:=L−1x, where C=LDL⊤C = LDL^\topC=LDL⊤. Then for i>ji > ji>j, Cov[x(i),x(j)∣x(1),…,x(j−1)]=ℓijdj,j=1,…,p−1(11) \text{Cov}[x^{(i)}, x^{(j)}|x^{(1)}, \dots, x^{(j-1)}] = \ell_{ij}d_j, \qquad j = 1, \dots, p-1 \tag{11} Cov[x(i),x(j)∣x(1),…,x(j−1)]=ℓij​dj​,j=1,…,p−1(11) where the j=1j=1j=1 case is interpreted as the unconditional covariance Cov[x(i),x(1)]\text{Cov}[x^{(i)},x^{(1)}]Cov[x(i),x(1)]. If we instead define LLL by C=LL⊤C = LL^\topC=LL⊤, then Cov[x(i),x(j)∣x(1),…,x(j−1)]=ℓijℓjj,j=1,…,p−1.(12) \text{Cov}[x^{(i)}, x^{(j)}|x^{(1)}, \dots, x^{(j-1)}] = \ell_{ij}\ell_{jj}, \qquad j = 1, \dots, p-1. \tag{12} Cov[x(i),x(j)∣x(1),…,x(j−1)]=ℓij​ℓjj​,j=1,…,p−1.(12) In particular, in either case we have ℓij=0  ⟺  Cov[x(i),x(j)∣x(1),…,x(j−1)]=0  ⟺  x(i)⊥x(j)∣x(1),…,x(j−1).(13) \ell_{ij}=0 \iff \text{Cov}[x^{(i)}, x^{(j)}|x^{(1)}, \dots, x^{(j-1)}] = 0 \iff x^{(i)} \perp x^{(j)} | x^{(1)}, \dots, x^{(j-1)}. \tag{13} ℓij​=0⟺Cov[x(i),x(j)∣x(1),…,x(j−1)]=0⟺x(i)⊥x(j)∣x(1),…,x(j−1).(13)

Proof. The proof proceeds similarly to the conditional variance case. We have \begin{align} \text{Cov}[x^{(i)}, x^{(j)}|x^{(1)}, \dots, x^{(j-1)}] &= \text{Cov}\left[\sum_{k=1}^{i} \ell_{ik} \epsilon^{(k)}, \sum_{k=1}^{j} \ell_{jk} \epsilon^{(k)} \bigg|x^{(1)}, \dots, x^{(j-1)}\right] \newline &= \text{Cov}\left[\sum_{k=1}^{i} \ell_{ik} \epsilon^{(k)}, \sum_{k=1}^{j} \ell_{jk} \epsilon^{(k)} \bigg|\epsilon^{(1)}, \dots, \epsilon^{(j-1)}\right] \newline &= \text{Cov}\left[\sum_{k=j}^{i} \ell_{ik} \epsilon^{(k)}, \ell_{jj} \epsilon^{(j)} \bigg|\epsilon^{(1)}, \dots, \epsilon^{(j-1)}\right] \newline &= \sum_{k=j}^{i} \ell_{ik}\ell_{jj} \text{Cov}\left[\epsilon^{(k)}, \epsilon^{(j)}|\epsilon^{(1)}, \dots, \epsilon^{(j-1)}\right] \newline &= \sum_{k=j}^{i} \ell_{ik}\ell_{jj} \text{Cov}\left[\epsilon^{(k)}, \epsilon^{(j)}\right] \newline &= \ell_{ij}\ell_{jj} \text{Var}\left[\epsilon^{(j)}\right] \end{align} The penultimate step uses the fact that the ϵ(j)\epsilon^{(j)}ϵ(j) are conditionally uncorrelated, owing to the fact that the ϵ(j)\epsilon^{(j)}ϵ(j) are jointly Gaussian and independent. The final step also uses the fact that the ϵ(j)\epsilon^{(j)}ϵ(j) are uncorrelated, and hence all terms where k≠jk \neq jk​=j vanish. For C=LDL⊤C = LDL^\topC=LDL⊤ the final expression simplifies to ℓijℓjjVar[ϵ(j)]=ℓij⋅1⋅dj=ℓijdj\ell_{ij}\ell_{jj} \text{Var}\left[\epsilon^{(j)}\right] = \ell_{ij} \cdot 1 \cdot d_j = \ell_{ij}d_jℓij​ℓjj​Var[ϵ(j)]=ℓij​⋅1⋅dj​=ℓij​dj​. For C=LL⊤C = LL^\topC=LL⊤ it becomes ℓijℓjj⋅1=ℓijℓjj\ell_{ij}\ell_{jj} \cdot 1 = \ell_{ij}\ell_{jj}ℓij​ℓjj​⋅1=ℓij​ℓjj​. The first implication in (13) follows immediately from (11) and (12). The second implication follows from the fact that xxx is Gaussian, and hence the conditional uncorrelatedness implies conditional independence. ■\blacksquare■

We thus find that the Cholesky decomposition of a Gaussian covariance is closely linked to the ordered conditional dependence structure of xxx. The factorization encodes conditional covariances, where the conditioning is with respect to all preceding variables; reordering the entries of xxx may yield drastically different insights. The connection between sparsity in the Cholesky factor and conditional independence can be leveraged in the design of statistical models and algorithms. For an example, see the paper (Jurek & Katzfuss, 2021).

A Regression Interpretation

In this section, we summarize a least squares regression interpretation of the modified Cholesky decomposition C=LDL⊤C = LDL^\topC=LDL⊤. The result is similar in spirit to (7), as we will consider a sequence of regressions that condition on previous entries of xxx. The ideas discussed here come primarily from from (Rothman et al., 2010).

Sequence of Least Squares Problems

We start by recursively defining a sequence of least squares problems, which we then link to the factorization C=LDL⊤C = LDL^\topC=LDL⊤.

Sequential Least Squares.
Let x∼N(0,C)x \sim \mathcal{N}(0,C)x∼N(0,C), with C=Cov[x]C = \text{Cov}[x]C=Cov[x] positive definite. We recursively define the entries of ϵ:=(ϵ(1),…,ϵ(p))\epsilon := (\epsilon^{(1)}, \dots, \epsilon^{(p)})ϵ:=(ϵ(1),…,ϵ(p)) as follows:

1. Set ϵ(1):=x(1)\epsilon^{(1)} := x^{(1)}ϵ(1):=x(1).
2. For j=2,…,pj = 2, \dots, pj=2,…,p define the regression coefficient β(j)∈Rj−1\beta^{(j)} \in \R^{j-1}β(j)∈Rj−1 by β(j):=argminβE∣x(j)−∑k=1j−1βkϵ(k)∣2(14) \beta^{(j)} := \text{argmin}_{\beta} \mathbb{E}\left\lvert x^{(j)} - \sum_{k=1}^{j-1} \beta_k \epsilon^{(k)} \right\rvert^2 \tag{14} β(j):=argminβ​E∣∣∣∣∣∣​x(j)−k=1∑j−1​βk​ϵ(k)∣∣∣∣∣∣​2(14) and set ϵ(j):=x(j)−∑k=1j−1βk(j)ϵ(k).(15) \epsilon^{(j)} := x^{(j)} - \sum_{k=1}^{j-1} \beta_k^{(j)} \epsilon^{(k)}. \tag{15} ϵ(j):=x(j)−k=1∑j−1​βk(j)​ϵ(k).(15)

In words, ϵ(j)\epsilon^{(j)}ϵ(j) is the residual of the least squares regression of the response x(j)x^{(j)}x(j) on the explanatory variables ϵ(1),…,ϵ(j−1)\epsilon^{(1)}, \dots, \epsilon^{(j-1)}ϵ(1),…,ϵ(j−1), and β(j)\beta^{(j)}β(j) is the coefficient vector. Take note that we are regressing on the residuals from the previous regressions, rather than the x(j)x^{(j)}x(j) themselves. We assume for simplicity that xxx is mean zero to avoid having to deal with an intercept term; for non mean zero variables, we can start by subtracting off their mean and then apply the same procedure. Note also that the zero mean assumption implies that E[ϵ]=0\mathbb{E}[\epsilon] = 0E[ϵ]=0; this follows from ϵ(1)=x(1)\epsilon^{(1)} = x^{(1)}ϵ(1)=x(1) along with the recursion (15).

Our goal is now to connect this algorithm to the modified Cholesky decomposition of CCC. In particular, we will show that the ϵ\epsilonϵ defined by the regression residuals is precisely the ϵ\epsilonϵ defined in (5), which arises from the modified Cholesky decomposition. To start, note that if we rearrange (15) as x(j):=ϵ(j)+∑k=1j−1β(k)ϵ(k),(16) x^{(j)} := \epsilon^{(j)} + \sum_{k=1}^{j-1} \beta^{(k)} \epsilon^{(k)}, \tag{16} x(j):=ϵ(j)+k=1∑j−1​β(k)ϵ(k),(16) then we see the vectors ϵ\epsilonϵ and xxx are related as x=Lϵ,(17) x = L\epsilon, \tag{17} x=Lϵ,(17) where \begin{align} L &:= \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \newline \beta^{(2)}_1 & 1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \newline \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \cdots & 0 \newline \beta^{(p)}_1 & \beta^{(p)}_2 & \cdots & \cdots & 1\end{bmatrix}. \tag{18} \end{align} That is, we have defined LLL to be the lower triangular matrix with jthj^{\text{th}}jth row set to (β(j),1)(\beta^{(j)}, 1)(β(j),1), the jthj^{\text{th}}jth coefficient vector with a 111 appended to the end. We immediately have that LLL is invertible, as it is a triangular matrix with non-zero entries on the diagonal. We also have C=Cov[x]=Cov[Lϵ]=LCov[ϵ]L⊤.(19) C = \text{Cov}[x] = \text{Cov}[L\epsilon] = L \text{Cov}[\epsilon] L^{\top}. \tag{19} C=Cov[x]=Cov[Lϵ]=LCov[ϵ]L⊤.(19) In order to show that (19) actually yields the modified Chokesky factorization, we must establish that Cov[ϵ]\text{Cov}[\epsilon]Cov[ϵ], the residual covariance matrix, is diagonal with positive diagonal entries.

Proposition.
The random vector ϵ\epsilonϵ defined by (12) satisfies ϵ∼N(0,D),(20) \epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, D), \tag{20} ϵ∼N(0,D),(20) where DDD is a diagonal matrix with positive entries on the diagonal.

Proof. The result follows immediately upon viewing (14) as a projection in a suitable inner product space, and then applying the Hilbert projection theorem. In particular, note that all of the x(j)x^{(j)}x(j) and ϵ(j)\epsilon^{(j)}ϵ(j) are zero mean, square integrable random variables. We can thus consider the Hilbert space of all such random variables with inner product defined by ⟨ψ,η⟩:=E[ψη]\langle \psi, \eta \rangle := \mathbb{E}[\psi \eta]⟨ψ,η⟩:=E[ψη]. Under this interpretation, we see that (15) can be rewritten as ∑k=1j−1βk(j)ϵ(k)=argminx′∈E(j)∥x−x′∥,(21) \sum_{k=1}^{j-1} \beta_k^{(j)} \epsilon^{(k)} = \text{argmin}_{x^\prime \in \mathcal{E}^{(j)}} \lVert x - x^\prime \rVert, \tag{21} k=1∑j−1​βk(j)​ϵ(k)=argminx′∈E(j)​∥x−x′∥,(21) where E(j)\mathcal{E}^{(j)}E(j) is the subspace spanned by ϵ(1),…,ϵ(j)\epsilon^{(1)}, \dots, \epsilon^{(j)}ϵ(1),…,ϵ(j) and ∥⋅∥\lVert \cdot \rVert∥⋅∥ is the norm induced by ⟨⋅,⋅⟩\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle⟨⋅,⋅⟩. Since ϵ(j)\epsilon^{(j)}ϵ(j) is the residual associated with the projection (21), the Hilbert projection theorem gives the optimality condition ϵ(j)⊥E(j)\epsilon^{(j)} \perp \mathcal{E}^{(j)}ϵ(j)⊥E(j); that is, ⟨ϵ(j),ϵ(k)⟩=E[ϵ(j)ϵ(k)]=Cov[ϵ(j),ϵ(k)]=0,k=1,…,j−1.(22) \langle \epsilon^{(j)}, \epsilon^{(k)} \rangle = \mathbb{E}[\epsilon^{(j)} \epsilon^{(k)}] = \text{Cov}[\epsilon^{(j)}, \epsilon^{(k)}] = 0, \qquad k = 1, \dots, j-1. \tag{22} ⟨ϵ(j),ϵ(k)⟩=E[ϵ(j)ϵ(k)]=Cov[ϵ(j),ϵ(k)]=0,k=1,…,j−1.(22) This implies that all of the residuals are pairwise uncorrelated, and hence D=Cov[ϵ]D = \text{Cov}[\epsilon]D=Cov[ϵ] is diagonal. We know from (17) that x=Lϵx = L\epsilonx=Lϵ; since C=Cov[x]C = \text{Cov}[x]C=Cov[x] is positive definite, then Cov[ϵ]\text{Cov}[\epsilon]Cov[ϵ] must also be positive definite. Thus, the diagonal entries of DDD must be strictly positive. ■\qquad \blacksquare■

Using the recursive regression procedure in (14) and (15), we have constructed ϵ\epsilonϵ and LLL satisfying C=LDL⊤C = LDL^\topC=LDL⊤, where D=Cov[ϵ]D = \text{Cov}[\epsilon]D=Cov[ϵ] is a diagonal matrix with positive diagonal entries, and LLL is lower triangular. By the uniqueness of the modified Cholesky decomposition (noted in the introduction) it follows that we have precisely formed the unique matrices LLL and DDD definining the modified Cholesky decomposition of CCC.
