Linearly Transforming Gaussian Process Priors

I derive how a linear transformation of a Gaussian process prior influences the Gaussian process posterior, and consider some special cases.

Published

06 March 2024

The main goal of this post is to apply different types of linear transformations to Gaussian vectors, and investigate how these transformations impact the resulting Gaussian conditional distributions. Since many Gaussian process (GP) derivations reduce to calculations with Gaussian vectors, our investigations of multivariate Gaussians will immediately lead to results on GPs. From the GP perspective, I emphasize that we will not be considering linear functionals of GPs (i.e., maps that take a function as input and return a scalar). This is an interesting topic that is worthy of its own blog post. We will instead be considering linear transformations applied in a pointwise fashion. We start by considering a generic multivariate Gaussian setup, then translate the results into GP language. We conclude by discussing applications to GP regression, multi-output GPs, and linear inverse problems.

Multivariate Gaussians

Notation and Review

We start by considering (finite-dimensional) multivariate Gaussians, as many of the derivations in the Gaussian process setting reduce to computations with Gaussian vectors. Consider a Gaussian vector x∼N(μ,C)x \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, C)x∼N(μ,C) partitioned as \begin{align} x = \begin{bmatrix} x_M \newline x_N \end{bmatrix} &\sim \mathcal{N}\left( \begin{bmatrix} \mu_M \newline \mu_N \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} C_M & C_{MN} \newline C_{NM} & C_N \end{bmatrix} \right) \tag{1} \end{align} where xM∈RMx_M \in \mathbb{R}^MxM​∈RM, xN∈RNx_N \in \mathbb{R}^NxN​∈RN, and CNM=CMN⊤C_{NM} = C_{MN}^\topCNM​=CMN⊤​. Throughout this post, subscripts with capital letters serve as indicators of vector and matrix dimensions. It is well-known that the conditional distribution xM∣xNx_M|x_NxM​∣xN​ is again Gaussian. In particular, we have xM∣xN∼N(μ^M,C^N)x_M|x_N \sim \mathcal{N}(\hat{\mu}_M, \hat{C}_N)xM​∣xN​∼N(μ^​M​,C^N​), where \begin{align} \hat{\mu}_M &:= \mu_M + C_{MN} C_{N}^{-1}(x_N - \mu_N) \tag{2} \newline \hat{C}_M &:= C_M - C_{MN} C_{N}^{-1} C_{NM}. \end{align}

Linear Transformation of Gaussian Vector

We recall that linear transformations of Gaussians preserve Gaussianity. Therefore, for a matrix A∈RR×(M+N)A \in \mathbb{R}^{R \times (M + N)}A∈RR×(M+N), the random vector y:=Axy := Axy:=Ax has distribution

\begin{align} y \sim \mathcal{N}(A\mu, ACA^\top). \tag{3} \end{align} In this post we will be concerned with matrices AAA with certain structure; the motivation will become more clear when we start considering GPs. For now, suppose that AAA is of the form \begin{align} A := \begin{bmatrix} A_M & 0 \newline 0 & A_N \end{bmatrix}, \tag{4} \end{align} where AM∈RR1×MA_M \in \mathbb{R}^{R_1 \times M}AM​∈RR1​×M and AN∈RR2×NA_N \in \mathbb{R}^{R_2 \times N}AN​∈RR2​×N. In this case, the transformed distribution (3) assumes the form \begin{align} y \sim \mathcal{N}\left( \begin{bmatrix} A_M\mu_M \newline A_N \mu_N \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} A_M C_M A_M^\top & A_M C_{MN} A_N^\top \newline A_N C_{NM}A_M^\top & A_N C_N A_N^\top \end{bmatrix} \right). \tag{5} \end{align} Having characterized the joint distribution of the transformed vector AxAxAx, we now consider the effect on the conditional distribution. Let yM:=AMxMy_M := A_M x_MyM​:=AM​xM​ and yN:=ANxNy_N := A_N x_NyN​:=AN​xN​. Applying the Gaussian conditioning identity (2), we obtain \begin{align} y_M | y_N \sim \mathcal{N}\left(\hat{\mu}_M^{y}, \hat{C}_M^{y} \right), \end{align}
where

\begin{align} \hat{\mu}_{M}^{y} &:= A_M \mu_M + A_M C_{MN} A_N^\top (A_N C_N A_N^\top)^{-1}[A_N x_N - A_N \mu_N] \tag{6} \newline \hat{C}_{M}^{y} &:= A_M C_M A_M^\top - A_M C_{MN} A_N^\top (A_N C_N A_N^\top)^{-1} A_N C_{NM} A_M^\top. \end{align}

Generalization to Affine Maps

The generalization from linear to affine maps is almost immediate. Consider an affine map of the form \begin{align} y := Ax + b &= \begin{bmatrix} A_M & 0 \newline 0 & A_N \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_M \newline x_N \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} b_M \newline b_N \end{bmatrix}. \tag{7} \end{align} The joint distribution of yyy is then given by
\begin{align} y \sim \mathcal{N}\left( \begin{bmatrix} A_M\mu_M + b_M \newline A_N \mu_N + b_N \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} A_M C_M A_M^\top & A_M C_{MN} A_N^\top \newline A_N C_{NM}A_M^\top & A_N C_N A_N^\top. \end{bmatrix} \right) \tag{8} \end{align} Note that the constant terms only affect the mean. Applying the Gaussian conditioning formulas then gives the conditional distribution \begin{align} y_M | y_N \sim \mathcal{N}\left(\hat{\mu}_M^{y}, \hat{C}_M^{y} \right), \end{align}
where

\begin{align} \hat{\mu}_{M}^{y} &:= b_M + A_M \mu_M + A_M C_{MN} A_N^\top (A_N C_N A_N^\top)^{-1}[A_N x_N - A_N \mu_N] \tag{9} \newline \hat{C}_{M}^{y} &:= A_M C_M A_M^\top - A_M C_{MN} A_N^\top (A_N C_N A_N^\top)^{-1} A_N C_{NM} A_M^\top. \end{align} The only difference with respect to (6) is the addition of bMb_MbM​ in the conditional mean. Note that the ANxN−ANμNA_N x_N - A_N \mu_NAN​xN​−AN​μN​ term in the conditional mean is unchanged due to the cancellation bM−bMb_M - b_MbM​−bM​.

Note that AMA_MAM​ and ANA_NAN​ map to RR1\mathbb{R}^{R_1}RR1​ and RR2\mathbb{R}^{R_2}RR2​, respectively. Therefore, the dimension of AxAxAx may differ from that of xxx. Now, it would be nice to be able to write μ^My\hat{\mu}_{M}^{y}μ^​My​ and C^My\hat{C}_{M}^{y}C^My​ as functions of μ^M\hat{\mu}_Mμ^​M​ and C^M\hat{C}_MC^M​, respectively. In the general setting, there is not much we can do given that ANA_NAN​ may not be invertible; thus, we can’t necessarily simplify the term (ANCNAN⊤)−1(A_N C_N A_N^\top)^{-1}(AN​CN​AN⊤​)−1.

Special case: Invertibility

Let’s now consider the special case that ANA_NAN​ is invertible; in particular, this means AN∈RN×NA_N \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}AN​∈RN×N. We’ll work with the affine map (7), since the linear result follows as the special case b=0b = 0b=0. With the invertibility assumption we can simplify (9) as \begin{align} \hat{\mu}_{M}^{y} &= b_M + A_M \mu_M + A_M C_{MN} A_N^\top (A_N^\top)^{-1} C_N^{-1} A_N^{-1} A_N[x_N - \mu_N] \tag{10} \newline &= b_M + A_M \left(\mu_M + C_{MN} C_N^{-1} [x_N - \mu_N] \right) \newline &= b_M + A_M \hat{\mu}_{M} \newline \hat{C}_{M}^{y} &= A_M C_M A_M^\top - A_M C_{MN} A_N^\top (A_N C_N A_N^\top)^{-1} A_N C_{NM} A_M^\top \newline &= A_M C_M A_M^\top - A_M C_{MN} A_N^\top (A_N^\top)^{-1} C_N^{-1} A_N^{-1} A_N C_{NM} A_M^\top \newline &= A_M \left(C_M - C_{MN} C_N^{-1} C_{NM}\right) A_M^\top \newline &= A_M \hat{C}_{M} A_M^\top. \end{align} In words, what we have just shown is that, if ANA_NAN​ is invertible, then conditioning yM∣yNy_M|y_NyM​∣yN​ is equivalent to conditioning xM∣xNx_M|x_NxM​∣xN​ and then applying the transformation AMA_MAM​ after the fact. We might write this symbolically as \begin{align} (A_M x_M | A_N x_N) \overset{d}{=} A_M(x_M | x_N). \tag{11} \end{align} This result makes intuitive sense; since ANA_NAN​ is a bijection, then conditioning on xNx_NxN​ is equivalent to conditioning on ANxNA_N x_NAN​xN​ - they both contain the same information. Note that no invertibility assumption is required for AMA_MAM​; what matters here is the variable that is being conditioned on. This equivalence does not necessarily hold when ANA_NAN​ is non-invertible. For example, consider the case that ANA_NAN​ has a single row. Then conditioning on ANxNA_N x_NAN​xN​ means that the observed quantity is a linear combination of the elements of xNx_NxN​. This quantity contains less information than observing each entry of xNx_NxN​ directly, since many different realizations of xNx_NxN​ might yield the same observed linear combination.

Gaussian Process Review

We now seek to apply the above results in the context of Gaussian processes (GP). This section provides a brief review of GPs, mainly intended to introduce notation.

Gaussian Process Prior

We consider a GP distribution over functions f:U→Rf: \mathcal{U} \to \mathbb{R}f:U→R with U⊂RD\mathcal{U} \subset \mathbb{R}^DU⊂RD. In particular, consider a GP f(⋅)∼GP(μ(⋅),k(⋅,⋅))f(\cdot) \sim \mathcal{GP}(\mu(\cdot), k(\cdot, \cdot))f(⋅)∼GP(μ(⋅),k(⋅,⋅)) with mean function μ:U→R\mu: \mathcal{U} \to \mathbb{R}μ:U→R and positive definite kernel (i.e., covariance function) k:U×U→Rk: \mathcal{U} \times \mathcal{U} \to \mathbb{R}k:U×U→R. Throughout this post we suppose that we have observed the function evaluations {un,f(un)}n=1N\{u_n, f(u_n)\}_{n=1}^{N}{un​,f(un​)}n=1N​ and seek to perform inference at a set of new inputs {u~m}m=1M\{\tilde{u}_m\}_{m=1}^{M}{u~m​}m=1M​. Echoing the notation from the previous sections we will let fN,μN∈RNf_N, \mu_N \in \mathbb{R}^{N}fN​,μN​∈RN denote the vectors defined by (fN)n:=f(un)(f_N)_n := f(u_n)(fN​)n​:=f(un​) and (μN)n:=μ(un)(\mu_N)_n := \mu(u_n)(μN​)n​:=μ(un​). We analogously define fM,μM∈RMf_M, \mu_M \in \mathbb{R}^MfM​,μM​∈RM to be the vectors containing the evaluations of f(⋅)f(\cdot)f(⋅) and μ(⋅)\mu(\cdot)μ(⋅) at the unobserved inputs u~1,…,u~M\tilde{u}_1, \dots, \tilde{u}_Mu~1​,…,u~M​. Finally, we let CN∈RN×NC_N \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}CN​∈RN×N, CM∈RM×MC_M \in \mathbb{R}^{M \times M}CM​∈RM×M, and CNM∈RN×MC_{NM} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times M}CNM​∈RN×M denote the matrices given by (CN)n,n′:=k(un,un′)(C_N)_{n,n^\prime} := k(u_n, u_{n^\prime})(CN​)n,n′​:=k(un​,un′​), (CM)m,m′:=k(u~m,u~m′)(C_M)_{m,m^\prime} := k(\tilde{u}_m, \tilde{u}_{m^\prime})(CM​)m,m′​:=k(u~m​,u~m′​), and (CNM)n,m:=k(un,u~m)(C_{NM})_{n,m} := k(u_n, \tilde{u}_m)(CNM​)n,m​:=k(un​,u~m​). We also define CMN:=CNM⊤C_{MN} := C_{NM}^\topCMN​:=CNM⊤​.

Gaussian Process Posterior

The vector fMf_MfM​ is unobserved and the goal is to characterize the conditional distribution fM∣fNf_M | f_NfM​∣fN​ (note that we will always be implicitly conditioning on the inputs). This distribution can be derived by considering the joint distribution implied by the GP prior: \begin{align} \begin{bmatrix} f_M \newline f_N \end{bmatrix} \sim \mathcal{N}\left(\begin{bmatrix} \mu_M \newline \mu_N \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} C_M \quad C_{MN} \newline C_{NM} \quad C_N \end{bmatrix} \right). \end{align} The Gaussian conditioning identities (2) imply that the conditional f^M:=fM∣fN\hat{f}_M := f_M|f_Nf^​M​:=fM​∣fN​ is also Gaussian f^M∼N(μ^M,C^M)\hat{f}_M \sim \mathcal{N}(\hat{\mu}_{M}, \hat{C}_{M})f^​M​∼N(μ^​M​,C^M​) with mean and covariance given by

\begin{align} \hat{\mu}_{M} &:= \mu_M + C_{MN} C_N^{-1} (f_N - \mu_N) \tag{12} \newline \hat{C}_{M} &:= C_M - C_{MN} C_N^{-1} C_{NM}. \end{align}

Since GPs are characterized by their finite-dimensional distributions, (12) implies that the conditional distribution over functions f^:=f∣fN\hat{f} := f|f_Nf^​:=f∣fN​ is also a GP. The formulas in (12) give the mean and covariance function of f^\hat{f}f^​, evaluated at the arbitrary inputs u~1,…,u~M\tilde{u}_1, \dots, \tilde{u}_Mu~1​,…,u~M​.

Pointwise Transformations of Gaussian Processes

We begin our GP applications with what I’m calling a “pointwise” affine transformation of the GP. Concretely, given a GP f∼GP(μ,k)f \sim \mathcal{GP}(\mu, k)f∼GP(μ,k) over the input space U\mathcal{U}U, we define the process \begin{align} &g: \mathcal{U} \to \mathbb{R}, &&g(u) := \alpha f(u) + \beta, \tag{13} \end{align} where 0≠α∈R0 \neq \alpha \in \mathbb{R}0​=α∈R and b∈Rb \in \mathbb{R}b∈R. In words, we are simply applying an invertible affine transformation to the GP output on a uuu-by-uuu basis. Letting gNg_NgN​, gMg_MgM​ be the analogs of fNf_NfN​, fMf_MfM​ for the g(⋅)g(\cdot)g(⋅) evaluations, we see that \begin{align} \begin{bmatrix} g_M \newline g_N \end{bmatrix} \sim \mathcal{N}\left(\begin{bmatrix} A_M \mu_M + b_M \newline A_N \mu_N + b_N \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} A_M C_M A_M^\top \quad A_M C_{MN} A_N^\top \newline A_N C_{NM} A_M^\top \quad A_N C_N A_N^\top \end{bmatrix} \right), \end{align} where \begin{align} &b_N := \beta 1_N, &&A_N := \text{diag}(\alpha, \dots, \alpha), \end{align} with 1N∈RN1_N \in \mathbb{R}^N1N​∈RN denoting a vector of ones. The quantities AMA_MAM​ and bMb_MbM​ are defined identically, with only the dimensions of the matrix and vector changing.

Under the assumption α≠0\alpha \neq 0α​=0, ANA_NAN​ is invertible so we are in the regime (10). Applying this result, we see that the conditional distribution is given by \begin{align} g_M | g_N &\sim \mathcal{N}(\hat{\mu}^g_M, \hat{C}^g_M), \end{align} where

\begin{align} &\hat{\mu}^g_M := A_M \hat{\mu}_M + b_M &&\hat{C}^g_M := A_M \hat{C}_M A_M^\top. \end{align}

In other words, we have shown \begin{align} \hat{g}(u) = \alpha \hat{f}(u) + \beta, \end{align} meaning that transforming the prior f(⋅)f(\cdot)f(⋅) in (13) is equivalent to applying the same transformation to the posterior f^\hat{f}f^​.
