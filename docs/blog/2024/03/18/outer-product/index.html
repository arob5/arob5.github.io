Generalizing the Outer Product to Hilbert Space

I briefly discuss how the outer product, familiar in Euclidean space, can be viewed as a linear operator which can readily be generalized to Hilbert space.

Published

18 March 2024

Finite Dimensional Euclidean Space

In Euclidean space, where x,y∈Rnx, y \in \mathbb{R}^nx,y∈Rn, the outer product is defined via the matrix multiplication xy⊤∈Rn×nxy^\top \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}xy⊤∈Rn×n. Since the resulting n×nn \times nn×n matrix can be viewed as a linear map, it is natural to consider the outer product as an operation which accepts two vectors and uses them to construct a linear map L(Rn,Rn)\mathcal{L}(\mathbb{R}^n, \mathbb{R}^n)L(Rn,Rn) (throughout this post, I use the notation L(H1,H2)\mathcal{L}(H_1, H_2)L(H1​,H2​) to denote the space of linear maps from a Hilbert space H1H_1H1​ to another Hilbert space H2H_2H2​). Given this viewpoint, let us denote this operator by ⊗(⋅,⋅):Rn×Rn→L(Rn,Rn), \otimes(\cdot, \cdot): \mathbb{R}^n \times \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathcal{L}(\mathbb{R}^n, \mathbb{R}^n), ⊗(⋅,⋅):Rn×Rn→L(Rn,Rn), though we will favor the binary operator notation x⊗y=⊗(x,y),x,y∈Rn. x \otimes y = \otimes(x, y), \qquad x, y \in \mathbb{R}^n. x⊗y=⊗(x,y),x,y∈Rn. Letting x,y,z∈Rnx, y, z \in \mathbb{R}^nx,y,z∈Rn, the defining relation of this operator can be uncovered by observing (x⊗y)(z)=(xy⊤)z=x(y⊤z)=x⟨y,z⟩, (x \otimes y)(z) = (xy^\top) z = x(y^\top z) = x \langle y, z \rangle, (x⊗y)(z)=(xy⊤)z=x(y⊤z)=x⟨y,z⟩, where associativity allowed us to re-write things in terms on an inner, rather than outer, product. While the definition x⊗y=xy⊤x \otimes y = xy^\topx⊗y=xy⊤ may not appear amenable to generalization in the case where xxx and yyy are infinite-dimensional vectors, the defining relation \begin{align} (x \otimes y)(z) &= \langle y, z \rangle x \tag{1} \end{align} only requires an inner product, and hence is readily generalizable.

Generalizing to Hilbert Space

We now consider a Hilbert space equipped with inner product ⟨⋅,⋅⟩.\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle.⟨⋅,⋅⟩. Given vectors x,y∈Hx, y \in Hx,y∈H, we define the operation x⊗y:H→Hx \otimes y: H \to Hx⊗y:H→H by

\begin{align} (x \otimes y)(z) &= \langle y, z \rangle x, \qquad z \in H \end{align}

thus generalizing (1). Given the constructive definition, this operator clearly exists and inherits linearity (in zzz) from the inner product; i.e., x⊗y∈L(H,H)x \otimes y \in \mathcal{L}(H, H)x⊗y∈L(H,H).

The operator x⊗yx \otimes yx⊗y is not unique in the sense that different choices of xxx and yyy can yield the same operator in L(H,H)\mathcal{L}(H, H)L(H,H); e.g., for α∈R\alpha \in \mathbb{R}α∈R, (αx⊗α−1y)(z)=⟨α−1y,z⟩(αx)=⟨y,z⟩x=(x⊗y)(z). (\alpha x \otimes \alpha^{-1} y)(z) = \langle \alpha^{-1} y, z \rangle (\alpha x) = \langle y, z \rangle x = (x \otimes y)(z). (αx⊗α−1y)(z)=⟨α−1y,z⟩(αx)=⟨y,z⟩x=(x⊗y)(z).

I’m not going to get into the applications of this operator here, but I will briefly note that (just like the familiar outer product) this operation is closely related to projection. If we set x=yx=yx=y then (x⊗x)(z)=⟨x,z⟩x, (x \otimes x)(z) = \langle x, z \rangle x, (x⊗x)(z)=⟨x,z⟩x, which is precisely the projection of zzz onto xxx (when xxx has unit norm).

Formulation via the Adjoint

While we have generalized the outer product via the defining relation (1), we might still wonder if we can make meaning of the expression x⊗y=xy⊤(2) x \otimes y = xy^\top \tag{2} x⊗y=xy⊤(2) when x,y∈Hx,y \in Hx,y∈H. This provides a direct definition of the operator x⊗yx \otimes yx⊗y rather than indirectly defining it through its action on z∈Hz \in Hz∈H. Our plan of attack will be to replace the transpose in (2) with the adjoint, x⊗y=xy∗,(3) x \otimes y = xy^*, \tag{3} x⊗y=xy∗,(3) but we still need to provide a precise interpretation of this statement.

We first recall that the adjoint (in Hilbert space; there is also a more general notation of adjoint in Banach space) of a linear map L∈L(H1,H2)L \in \mathcal{L}(H_1, H_2)L∈L(H1​,H2​) is a linear map in the reverse direction, denoted L∗∈L(H2,H1)L^* \in \mathcal{L}(H_2, H_1)L∗∈L(H2​,H1​), satisfying the defining relation \begin{align} \langle Lx_1, x_2 \rangle_{H_2} &= \langle x_1, L^* x_2 \rangle_{H_1}, \qquad x_1 \in H_1, x_2 \in H_2. \tag{4} \end{align}

Note that the adjoint is defined for a linear operator, while in (3) we have written y∗y^*y∗ where y∈Hy \in Hy∈H is just a vector. The key here will be associating xxx and yyy with suitable linear operators. When working with inner products, it is common to associate a vector x∈Hx \in Hx∈H with the linear functional ℓx(z)=⟨x,z⟩\ell_x(z) = \langle x, z \rangleℓx​(z)=⟨x,z⟩. Instead of viewing xxx as the map ℓx∈L(H,R)\ell_x \in \mathcal{L}(H, \mathbb{R})ℓx​∈L(H,R), we will view it as the map Lx∈L(R,H)L_x \in \mathcal{L}(\mathbb{R}, H)Lx​∈L(R,H) defined by \begin{align} L_x(\alpha) &= \alpha x, \qquad \alpha \in \mathbb{R}. \end{align}

I claim that the adjoint Lx∗∈L(H,R)L^*_x \in \mathcal{L}(H, \mathbb{R})Lx∗​∈L(H,R) is given by \begin{align} L^*_x z = \langle x, z \rangle, \qquad z \in H. \tag{5} \end{align} Indeed, by definition (4), the adjoint satisfies

Summary

In this post, we generalized the outer product x⊗y=xy⊤x \otimes y = xy^\topx⊗y=xy⊤ on Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn to Hilbert space in two different, but equivalent, ways. Both approaches rely on viewing x⊗yx \otimes yx⊗y as a linear operator living in L(H,H)\mathcal{L}(H, H)L(H,H). The first approach indirectly defines x⊗yx \otimes yx⊗y through its action on other vectors zzz and resulted in the defining relation (1). The second directly generalizes xy⊤xy^\topxy⊤ from the finite-dimensional case by replacing the transpose with the adjoint, and viewing the expression xy∗xy^*xy∗ formally, with the underlying rigorous interpretation given by (6).
