Gaussian Measures, Part 2 - The Multivariate Case

Preliminaries

Sigma Algebra

We recall from the previous post that a univariate Gaussian measure is defined on the Borel σ\sigmaσ-algebra B(R)\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})B(R). Analogously, we will define an nnn-dimensional Gaussian measure on the Borel sets B(Rn)\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}^n)B(Rn). There are two reasonable approaches to define B(Rn)\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}^n)B(Rn), and I want to take a moment to highlight them since the same two options will present themselves when we consider defining the Borel sets over infinite-dimensional spaces.

Option 1: Leverage the Standard Topology on Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn

A Borel σ\sigmaσ-algebra can be defined for any space that comes equipped with a topology; i.e., a collection of open sets. The Borel σ\sigmaσ-algebra is then defined as the smallest σ\sigmaσ-algebra that contains all of these open sets. In the present setting, this means B(Rn):=σ{O⊆Rn:O is open},(1) \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}^n) := \sigma\left\{\mathcal{O} \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n : \mathcal{O} \text{ is open} \right\}, \tag{1} B(Rn):=σ{O⊆Rn:O is open},(1) where σ(S)\sigma(\mathcal{S})σ(S) denotes the σ\sigmaσ-algebra generated by a collection of sets S\mathcal{S}S. A nice perspective on B(Rn)\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}^n)B(Rn) is that it is the smallest σ\sigmaσ-algebra that ensures all continuous functions f:Rn→Rf: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}f:Rn→R are measurable. We note that this is not a property of Borel σ\sigmaσ-algebras more generally, but one that does hold in the special case of Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn; see this StackExchange post for some details.

Option 2: Product of One-Dimensional Borel Sets

A second reasonable approach is to try extending what we have already defined in one dimension, which means simply taking Cartesian products of one-dimensional Borel sets: B(Rn):=σ{B1×⋯×Bn:Bi∈B(R), i=1,…,n}.(2) \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}^n) := \sigma\left\{B_1 \times \cdots \times B_n: B_i \in \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}), \ i = 1, \dots, n \right\}. \tag{2} B(Rn):=σ{B1​×⋯×Bn​:Bi​∈B(R), i=1,…,n}.(2) It turns out that the resulting σ\sigmaσ-algebra agrees with that defined in option 1, so there is no ambiguity in the notation.

Definition: One-Dimensional Projections

With the σ\sigmaσ-algebra defined, we now consider how to define a Gaussian measure μ\muμ on the measurable space (Rn,B(Rn))(\mathbb{R}^n, \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}^n))(Rn,B(Rn)). We will explore a few different equivalent definitions, starting with this: a measure is Gaussian if all of its one-dimensional projections are (univariate) Gaussians.

Definition. A probability measure μ\muμ defined on the Borel measurable space (Rn,B(Rn))(\mathbb{R}^n, \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}^n))(Rn,B(Rn)) is called Gaussian if, for all linear maps ℓ∈(Rn)∗\ell \in (\mathbb{R}^n)^*ℓ∈(Rn)∗, the pushforward measure μ∘ℓ−1\mu \circ \ell^{-1}μ∘ℓ−1 is Gaussian on (R,B(R))(\mathbb{R}, \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}))(R,B(R)).

As in the univariate setting, we define a random variable XXX as Gaussian if its law XXX is a Gaussian measure. Recall that each linear map ℓ\ellℓ can be identified with a unique y∈Rny \in \mathbb{R}^ny∈Rn such that ℓ(x)=⟨x,y⟩\ell(x) = \langle x, y \rangleℓ(x)=⟨x,y⟩, which we indicate by writing ℓy=ℓ\ell_y = \ellℓy​=ℓ. Fourier Transform

Just as in the univariate case, the Fourier transform μ^\hat{\mu}μ^​ provides an alternate, equivalent, characterization of Gaussian measures. First, we recall how such a Fourier transform is defined in the multiple variable setting.

Definition. Let μ\muμ be a measure on (Rn,B(Rn))(\mathbb{R}^n, \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}^n))(Rn,B(Rn)). Then the Fourier transform of μ\muμ is defined as μ^(y):=∫Rnei⟨x,y⟩μ(dx),y∈Rn(3) \hat{\mu}(y) := \int_{\mathbb{R}^n} e^{i\langle x, y\rangle} \mu(dx), \qquad y \in \mathbb{R}^n \tag{3} μ^​(y):=∫Rn​ei⟨x,y⟩μ(dx),y∈Rn(3)

We can alternatively view μ^\hat{\mu}μ^​ as a function of ℓy∈(Rn)∗\ell_y \in (\mathbb{R}^n)^*ℓy​∈(Rn)∗; that is, ℓy↦∫Rneiℓy(x)μ(dx). \ell_y \mapsto \int_{\mathbb{R}^n} e^{i \ell_y(x)} \mu(dx). ℓy​↦∫Rn​eiℓy​(x)μ(dx). Note that this is similar in spirit to the definition of the nnn-dimensional Gaussian measure, in the sense that the extension from one to multiple dimensions is acheived by considering one-dimensional linear projections. With this background established, we can state the following, which gives an alternate definition of Gaussian measures.

Theorem. A probability measure μ\muμ defined on the Borel measurable space (Rn,B(Rn))(\mathbb{R}^n, \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}^n))(Rn,B(Rn)) is Gaussian if and only if its Fourier transform is of the form μ^(y)=exp⁡{i⟨m,y⟩−12⟨Cy,y⟩},(4) \hat{\mu}(y) = \exp\left\{i\langle m, y\rangle - \frac{1}{2}\langle Cy, y\rangle \right\}, \tag{4} μ^​(y)=exp{i⟨m,y⟩−21​⟨Cy,y⟩},(4) for some fixed vector m∈Rnm \in \mathbb{R}^{n}m∈Rn and symmetric, positive semi-definite matrix C∈Rn×nC \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}C∈Rn×n.

The proof, which is given in the appendix, also provides the expressions for the mean and covariance of μ\muμ as a byproduct.

Density Function

The one-dimensional projections and Fourier transform provide equivalent definitions of multivariate Gaussian measures. The more familiar notion of the Gaussian density provides a third characterization, with the caveat that it only pertains to the case that the covariance matrix $C$ is positive definite.

Proposition. Let $\mu$ be a Gaussian measure with mean vector $m$ and covariance matrix $C$, as in (5). Then $\mu$ admits a Lebesgue density if and only if $C$ is positive definite, in which case $$ \frac{d\mu}{d\lambda}(x) = \text{det}(2\pi C)^{-1/2}\exp\left\{-\frac{1}{2} \langle C^{-1}(x-m), x-m\rangle\right\}. \tag{6} $$

Transformation of Standard Gaussian Random Variables

In this section we provide yet another characterization of Gaussian measures. We consider a generative perspective, whereby a Gaussian random vector $X \in \mathbb{R}^n$ arises via a linear transformation of $n$ iid $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ random variables.

Proposition. Let $Z_1, \dots, Z_n$ be iid $\mathcal{N}(0, 1)$ random variables stacked into the column vector $Z \in \mathbb{R}^n$. Then, for any fixed vector $m \in \mathbb{R}^n$ and matrix $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$, the random variable given by $$ X := m + AZ \tag{7} $$ has a Gaussian distribution $\mathcal{N}(m, AA^\top)$. Conversely, let $X \in \mathbb{R}^n$ be a Gaussian random variable. Then there exists a vector $m \in \mathbb{R}^n$ and matrix $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ such that $X = m + AZ$.

Another way to think about this is that we have defined a transport map T:Rn→RnT: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^nT:Rn→Rn such that \begin{align} T(Z) &= X, &&\text{where } T(z) = m + Az. \end{align} That is, we feed in vectors with iid standard Gaussian components, and get out vectors with distribution N(m,AA⊤)\mathcal{N}(m, AA^\top)N(m,AA⊤). This is a very practical way to look at multivariate Gaussians, immediately providing the basis for a sampling algorithm. Indeed, suppose we want to draw iid samples from the distribution N(m,C)\mathcal{N}(m, C)N(m,C). Then the above proposition gives us a way to do so, provided that we can (1) draw univariate N(0,1)\mathcal{N}(0,1)N(0,1) samples; and (2) factorize the matrix CCC as C=AA⊤C = AA^\topC=AA⊤ for some AAA. This procedure is summarized in the below corollary.

Corollary. The following algorithm produces a sample from the distribution N(m,C)\mathcal{N}(m, C)N(m,C).
1. Draw nnn iid samples Zi∼N(0,1)Z_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)Zi​∼N(0,1) and stack them in a column vector ZZZ.
2. Compute a factorization C=AA⊤C = AA^\topC=AA⊤.
3. Return m+AZm + AZm+AZ.
Repeating steps 1 and 3 will produce independent samples from N(m,C)\mathcal{N}(m, C)N(m,C) (the matrix factorization need not be re-computed each time).

As for the factorization, the Cholesky decomposition is a standard choice when CCC is positive definite. When CCC is merely positive semidefinite, the eigendecomposition provides another option, since C=UDU⊤=UD1/2D1/2U⊤=(UD1/2)(UD1/2)⊤ C = UDU^\top = UD^{1/2} D^{1/2} U^\top = (UD^{1/2})(UD^{1/2})^\top C=UDU⊤=UD1/2D1/2U⊤=(UD1/2)(UD1/2)⊤ so setting A:=UD1/2A := UD^{1/2}A:=UD1/2 does the trick. Note that CCC is positive semidefinite so DDD is just a diagonal matrix with nonnegative values on the diagonal.

Finally, note that we can expand (7) as
\begin{equation} X = m + \sum_{i=1}^{n} Z_i A_i, \end{equation} where the AiA_iAi​ are the columns of AAA. This shows that we are representing the random vector XXX as “randomized summation”; that is, a linear combination of nonrandom vectors AiA_iAi​ where the coefficients ZiZ_iZi​ are random. In the case of the eigendecomposition, this summation assumes the form \begin{equation} X = m + \sum_{i=1}^{n} \sqrt{\lambda}_i Z_i U_i, \tag{8} \end{equation} where the λi\lambda_iλi​ are the diagonal values of DDD. This expression can be viewed as a special case of the Karhunen-Loeve expansion (Alexanderian, 2015).

Covariance Operator

As shown in (5) (and derived in the appendix), the covariance matrix associated with a Gaussian measure μ\muμ satisfies \begin{align} C &= \int (x - m)(x - m)^\top \mu(dx), \end{align} where mmm and CCC are the quantities given in the Fourier transform (4). We take a step further in this section by viewing the covariance as an operator rather than a matrix. Definitions of the covariance operator differ slightly across various textbooks and literature; we will try to touch on the different conventions here and explain their connections. As a starting point, we consider the following definition.

Definition. Let μ\muμ be a Gaussian measure with Fourier transform given by (4). Inner Products

One feature that makes the covariance operator a convenient mathematical object to study is the inner product structure it provides. Indeed, the following result states that the covariance operator is almost an inner product, and is a true inner product when the covariance matrix CCC is positive definite.

Proposition. Let μ\muμ be a Gaussian measure with Fourier transform given by (4). Then the covariance operator (9) is symmetric, bilinear, and positive semidefinite. If CCC, the covariance matrix of μ\muμ, is positive definite, then the covariance operator is also positive definite and thus defines an inner product.

Proof. Bilinearity follows immediately from definition (9). Symmetry similarly follows, and is more immediately obvious in expression (10). Since CCC is positive semidefinite, then C(y,y)=⟨Cy,y⟩≥0, \mathcal{C}(y,y) = \langle Cy, y\rangle \geq 0, C(y,y)=⟨Cy,y⟩≥0, so C\mathcal{C}C is also positive semidefinite. The inequality is strict when CCC is positive definite and y≠0y \neq 0y​=0, in which case C(⋅,⋅)\mathcal{C}(\cdot, \cdot)C(⋅,⋅) is an inner product. ■\qquad \blacksquare■

We can therefore think of C\mathcal{C}C as defining a new inner product by weighting the Euclidean inner product by a positive definite matrix CCC.

A Closely Related Operator

Our definition for the covariance operator C\mathcal{C}C arises form a looking at the quadratic form ⟨Cy,y⟩\langle Cy, y\rangle⟨Cy,y⟩ (the expression that appears in the Fourier transform) in a new way. In particular, we viewed this as a function of two arguments, such that the above quadratic form is the value the function takes when both arguments happen to be yyy. We could look at this from yet another perspective by considering the quadratic form as a function of only one of its arguments, say, the left one. This gives another useful operator that is closely related to C\mathcal{C}C.

At this point, the definition of C′\mathcal{C}^\primeC′ seems rather unnecessary given its similarity to CCC. As mentioned above, definitions of the covariance operator vary slightly in the literature. One basic modification commonly seen is to assume that μ\muμ is centered (zero mean) and thus define the covariance operator as C(y,y′):=∫⟨y,x⟩⟨y′,x⟩μ(dx).(14) \mathcal{C}(y, y^\prime) := \int \langle y, x\rangle \langle y^\prime, x\rangle \mu(dx). \tag{14} C(y,y′):=∫⟨y,x⟩⟨y′,x⟩μ(dx).(14) This is done primarily for convenience, as one can always center a Gaussian measure and then add back the mean when needed.
