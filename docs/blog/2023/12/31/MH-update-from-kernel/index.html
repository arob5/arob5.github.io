Deriving the Metropolis-Hastings Update from the Transition Kernel

Given only the Metroplis-Hastings transition kernel, I show how to recover the Metropolis-Hastings update rule.

Published

31 December 2023

The Metropolis-Hastings (MH) Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) method is typically introduced in the form of a practical algorithm. In a more theoretically-oriented context, one might prove that the algorithm defines a Markov chain and derive the associated transition (i.e. probability) kernel. I have found it insightful to also work through the derivations in the reverse order; given only the transition kernel, how could one derive the well-known MH update? In other words, how can you simulate the Markov chain implied by the transition kernel? In this post, I work through the required derivations.

Let Q:X×B→[0,1]Q: \mathcal{X} \times \mathcal{B} \to [0,1]Q:X×B→[0,1] denote the proposal kernel for the MH algorithm, with q(x,⋅)q(\mathbf{x}, \cdot)q(x,⋅) the Lebesgue density of the measure Q(x,⋅)Q(\mathbf{x}, \cdot)Q(x,⋅). Mixture of Kernels

Suppose we don’t already know the MH algorithm, and are given the task of writing an algorithm to draw a sample from the distribution P(x,⋅)P(\mathbf{x},\cdot)P(x,⋅) defined in (1). A reasonable place to start is to try writing P(x,⋅)P(\mathbf{x},\cdot)P(x,⋅) as a mixture of kernels from which we already know how to sample. Let’s first try this idea, attempting to write P(x,A)=wP1(x,A)+(1−w)P2(x,A),w∈[0,1],(3) P(\mathbf{x},A) = wP_1(\mathbf{x},A) + (1-w)P_2(\mathbf{x},A), \qquad w \in [0,1], \tag{3} P(x,A)=wP1​(x,A)+(1−w)P2​(x,A),w∈[0,1],(3) with P1P_1P1​, P2P_2P2​ transition kernels we already know how to sample from. If we are able to do this, then we could easily sample from P(x,⋅)P(\mathbf{x},\cdot)P(x,⋅) via the following simple algorithm:

  1. Select P1P_1P1​ with probability www, else select P2P_2P2​.
  2. Sample from the selected kernel.

To this end, let’s manipulate (1) to write it in the form of a kernel mixture. We have \begin{align} P(\mathbf{x},A) &= \int_A q(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})\alpha(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})d\mathbf{y} + \delta_{\mathbf{x}}(A) \int_{\mathcal{X}} q(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})[1-\alpha(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})] d\mathbf{y} \newline &= \overline{a}(\mathbf{x}) \int_A \frac{q(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})\alpha(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})}{\overline{a}(\mathbf{x})}d\mathbf{y} + \left[1-\overline{a}(\mathbf{x})\right] \delta_{\mathbf{x}}(A) \tag{4} \end{align} which is a kernel mixture of the form (3) with
\begin{align} w = \overline{a}(\mathbf{x}), \qquad P_1(\mathbf{x},A)=\int_A \frac{q(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})\alpha(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})}{\overline{a}(\mathbf{x})}d\mathbf{y}, \qquad P_2(\mathbf{x},A)=\delta_{\mathbf{x}}(A). \end{align} All we did here was to multiply and divide by the acceptance probability a‾(x)\overline{a}(\mathbf{x})a(x) in the first term (under typical assumptions on QQQ this will be non-zero when y\mathbf{y}y is in the support of π\piπ) and to rearrange the second term using the fact that q(x,⋅)q(\mathbf{x},\cdot)q(x,⋅) is a probability density; hence, ∫Xq(x,y)dy=1. \int_{\mathcal{X}} q(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) d\mathbf{y} = 1. ∫X​q(x,y)dy=1. Note that the mixture weight a‾(x)\overline{a}(\mathbf{x})a(x) is the overall acceptance probability, and is thus in [0,1][0,1][0,1] as required. Moreover, P2P_2P2​ is the Dirac measure centered at x\mathbf{x}x and is thus a valid kernel. To check that P1(x,⋅)P_1(\mathbf{x},\cdot)P1​(x,⋅) is a valid probability measure, we recall the form of a‾(x)\overline{a}(\mathbf{x})a(x) from (2) to verify that \begin{align} P_1(\mathbf{x},A) &= \int_{\mathcal{X}} \frac{q(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})\alpha(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})}{\overline{a}(\mathbf{x})}d\mathbf{y} \newline &= \frac{\int_{\mathcal{X}} q(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})\alpha(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})d\mathbf{y}}{\int_{\mathcal{X}} q(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})\alpha(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})d\mathbf{y}} \newline &= 1. \end{align} The other required properties (countable additivity and non-negativity) are similarly verified. Thus, P1(x,⋅)P_1(\mathbf{x},\cdot)P1​(x,⋅) is a probability measure with Lebesgue density proportional to q(x,y)α(x,y)q(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})\alpha(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})q(x,y)α(x,y); the overall acceptance probability a‾(x)\overline{a}(\mathbf{x})a(x) is the normalizing constant for this density.

This representation of the MH kernel as a mixture distribution is conceptually useful, but it does not directly help us determine a sampling algorithm. Indeed, we cannot implement the simple mixture sampling algorithm described above since (i.) computing the mixture weight a‾(x)\overline{a}(\mathbf{x})a(x) requires evaluating an intractable integral, and (ii.) we don’t know how to directly sample from the probability distribution with density proportional to q(x,y)α(x,y)q(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})\alpha(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})q(x,y)α(x,y). While this approach seems to be a dead end from a practical point of view, we should keep in mind that the MH algorithm derived below does sample from the mixture (4), but does so in a way that avoids having to compute the mixture weight or to directly sample from P1(x,⋅)P_1(\mathbf{x},\cdot)P1​(x,⋅).

Marginalized Mixture of Kernels

In the previous section, we feigned ignorance of the MH algorithm in order to approach the problem of simulating (1) as a generic sampling problem. We found that PPP can indeed be written as a mixture of kernels, but the problem of sampling from the resulting mixture was also intractable. To take a step in the right direction, it is useful to cheat a little bit and recall some of the mechanics of the MH algorithm. The proposal y∼Q(x,⋅)\mathbf{y} \sim Q(\mathbf{x},\cdot)y∼Q(x,⋅) is accepted with probability α(x,y)\alpha(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})α(x,y); if rejected, the next state is set to the current state x\mathbf{x}x. Thus, it seems that we should be looking for a mixture of kernels of the form α(x,y)δy(⋅)+[1−α(x,y)]δx(⋅).(5) \alpha(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})\delta_{\mathbf{y}}(\cdot) + [1-\alpha(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})]\delta_{\mathbf{x}}(\cdot). \tag{5} α(x,y)δy​(⋅)+[1−α(x,y)]δx​(⋅).(5) Of course, this can’t represent the whole picture since the mixture weight in (5) depends on y\mathbf{y}y and the proposal kernel QQQ is completely missing from the expression. The key insight is that the MH kernel P(x,⋅)P(\mathbf{x},\cdot)P(x,⋅) can be viewed as the expectation of (5) averaged with respect to Q(x,⋅)Q(\mathbf{x},\cdot)Q(x,⋅); i.e., P(x,⋅)P(\mathbf{x},\cdot)P(x,⋅) is derived by marginalizing the mixture (5) with respect to y∼Q(x,⋅)\mathbf{y} \sim Q(\mathbf{x},\cdot)y∼Q(x,⋅). To show this, we return to the original expression (1) for the MH kernel. We have \begin{align} P(\mathbf{x},A) &= \int_A q(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})\alpha(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})d\mathbf{y} + \delta_{\mathbf{x}}(A) \int_{\mathcal{X}} q(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})[1-\alpha(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})] d\mathbf{y} \newline &= \int_{\mathcal{X}} \alpha(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) \mathbf{1}(\mathbf{y} \in A) q(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) d\mathbf{y} + \int_{\mathcal{X}} [1-\alpha(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})] \delta_{\mathbf{x}}(A) q(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) d\mathbf{y} \newline &= \int_{\mathcal{X}} \alpha(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) \delta_{\mathbf{y}}(A) q(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) d\mathbf{y} + \int_{\mathcal{X}} [1-\alpha(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})] \delta_{\mathbf{x}}(A) q(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) d\mathbf{y} \newline &= \int_{\mathcal{X}} \left[\alpha(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})\delta_{\mathbf{y}}(A) + [1-\alpha(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})] \delta_{\mathbf{x}}(A) \right] q(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) d\mathbf{y}, \tag{6} \end{align} which is precisely the mixture (5) averaged (in y\mathbf{y}y) with respect to the weights q(x,y)q(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})q(x,y). We now have three different representations of the MH transition kernel PPP: (1) is the most natural to derive when starting from the MH algorithm, (4) represents PPP as a mixture of two distributions, and (6) represents PPP as a marginalized mixture of two distributions. It is this final representation which proves useful for developing a practical simulation algorithm.

All that remains is to recall how to sample from a marginalized distribution. First note that x\mathbf{x}x is essentially a fixed parameter in the integral (6); the averaging is done with respect to y\mathbf{y}y. Now, if we condition on a fixed y\mathbf{y}y as well, then the expression α(x,y)δy(A)+[1−α(x,y)]δx(A) \alpha(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})\delta_{\mathbf{y}}(A) + [1-\alpha(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})] \delta_{\mathbf{x}}(A) α(x,y)δy​(A)+[1−α(x,y)]δx​(A) is simply a mixture of two distributions, which we know how to sample from. Thus, a sample can be drawn from P(x,⋅)P(\mathbf{x},\cdot)P(x,⋅) via the following algorithm:

  1. Sample y∼Q(x,⋅)\mathbf{y} \sim Q(\mathbf{x}, \cdot)y∼Q(x,⋅).
  2. Conditional on y\mathbf{y}y, sample from α(x,y)δy+[1−α(x,y)]δx\alpha(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})\delta_{\mathbf{y}} + [1-\alpha(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})] \delta_{\mathbf{x}}α(x,y)δy​+[1−α(x,y)]δx​.

For the second step, the mixture can be sampled from using the simple algorithm discussed in the previous section: randomly select one of the two kernels with probabilities equal to their mixture weights, then return a sample from the selected kernel. Since sampling from the Dirac measure δx\delta_{\mathbf{x}}δx​ simply means returning the value x\mathbf{x}x (and similarly for δy\delta_{\mathbf{y}}δy​) then this step will simply return x\mathbf{x}x or y\mathbf{y}y according to their respective probabilities α(x,y)\alpha(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})α(x,y) and 1−α(x,y)1-\alpha(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})1−α(x,y). This is precisely the MH accept-reject mechanism!

Conclusion

While all of this might appear to be overcomplicating the very simply MH algorithm, I have found it a quite worthwhile exercise to contemplate some different perspectives on the method, as well as to get some practice manipulating expressions involving probability kernels and thinking through sampling schemes. The MH transition kernel (1) can easily be derived by thinking through the mechanics of the MH algorithm. In this post, I showed in (4) how the kernel can be re-written as mixture of two distributions and in (6) as a marginalized mixture of two distributions. It is this final expression which provides the basis for a tractable simulation algorithm.
