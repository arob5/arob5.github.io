Precision Matrices, Partial Correlations, and Conditional Independence

Published

11 January 2025

In this post, we explore how the precision (inverse covariance) matrix for a Gaussian distribution encodes conditional dependence relations between the underlying variables. We also introduce the notions of conditional and partial correlation, and prove their equivalence in the Gaussian setting. Ideas along these lines are widely used in areas such as probabilistic graphical models (e.g., Gaussian Markov random fields and scalable Gaussian processes) and high-dimensional covariance estimation. A natural complement to this topic is my post on the Cholesky decomposition of a covariance matrix, which provides an alternate route to analyzing conditional dependence structure. The main source for this post is (Lauritzen, 2019), which is freely available online and provides an excellent rigorous introduction to graphical models in statistics.

Setup and Notation

Throughout this post we consider a set of random variables x1,…,xnx_1, \dots, x_nx1​,…,xn​, each taking values in R\mathbb{R}R. For an (ordered) index set A⊆{1,…,n}A \subseteq \{1,\dots,n\}A⊆{1,…,n}, we write xAx_AxA​ to denote the column vector [xi]i∈A[x_i]_{i \in A}[xi​]i∈A​ that retains the ordering of AAA. We will use the convention that B:={1,…,n}∖AB := \{1,\dots,n\} \setminus AB:={1,…,n}∖A is the complement of AAA, and use the shorthand x∼ix_{\sim i}x∼i​ for the vector of all variables excluding xix_ixi​. We also write xi⊥xj∣xBx_i \perp x_j|x_Bxi​⊥xj​∣xB​ to mean that xix_ixi​ and xjx_jxj​ are conditionally independent given xBx_BxB​.

The Precision Matrix of a Gaussian

In this section we will explore how the precision matrix of a Gaussian distribution is closely related to the conditional dependence structure of the random variables x1,…,xnx_1, \dots, x_nx1​,…,xn​. Throughout this section, we assume x=(x1,…,xn)⊤∼N(m,C),(1) x = (x_1, \dots, x_n)^\top \sim \mathcal{N}(m, C), \tag{1} x=(x1​,…,xn​)⊤∼N(m,C),(1) where CCC is positive definite. Hence, it is invertible, and we denote its inverse by P:=C−1,(2) P := C^{-1}, \tag{2} P:=C−1,(2) which we refer to as the precision matrix. The precision inherits positive definiteness from CCC. In some contexts (e.g., (Lauritzen, 2019)), PPP is also called the concentration matrix.

Throughout this section, our focus will be on the dependence between two variables xix_ixi​ and xjx_jxj​, conditional on all others. Thus, let’s define the index sets A:={i,j}A := \{i,j\}A:={i,j} and B:={1,…,n}∖{i,j}B := \{1,\dots,n\} \setminus \{i,j\}B:={1,…,n}∖{i,j}. We partition the joint Gaussian (1) (after possibly reordering the variables) as \begin{align} \begin{bmatrix} x_A \newline x_B \end{bmatrix} &\sim \mathcal{N}\left( \begin{bmatrix} m_A \newline m_B \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} C_A & C_{AB} \newline C_{BA} & C_B \end{bmatrix} \right). \tag{3} \end{align}

Our focus is thus on the conditional distribution of xA∣xBx_A|x_BxA​∣xB​. We recall that conditionals of Gaussians are themselves Gaussian, and that the conditional covariance takes the form CA∣B:=Cov[xA∣xB]=CA−CABCB−1CBA.(4) C_{A|B} := \text{Cov}[x_A|x_B] = C_A - C_{AB}C_B^{-1}C_{BA}. \tag{4} CA∣B​:=Cov[xA​∣xB​]=CA​−CAB​CB−1​CBA​.(4) I go through these derivations in depth in this post. For our present purposes, it is important to appreciate the connection between the conditional covariance (4) and the joint precision PPP. To this end, let’s consider partitioning the precision in the same manner as the covariance: \begin{align} P &= C^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} P_A & P_{AB} \newline P_{BA} & P_B \end{bmatrix}. \tag{5} \end{align} The above blocks of PPP can be obtained via a direct application of partitioned matrix inverse identities from linear algebra (see, e.g., James E. Pustejovsky’s post for some nice background). Applying the partitioned matrix identity to (5) yields \begin{align} P_A &= (C_A - C_{AB}C_B^{-1}C_{BA})^{-1} \tag{6} \newline P_{AB} &= -C_B^{-1} C_{BA}P_A. \tag{7} \end{align} Notice in (6) that PAP_APA​, the upper-left block of the joint precision, is precisely equal to the inverse of the conditional covariance CA∣BC_{A|B}CA∣B​ given in (4). We denote this conditional precision by PA∣B:=(CA∣B)−1.(8) P_{A|B} := (C_{A|B})^{-1}. \tag{8} PA∣B​:=(CA∣B​)−1.(8) We summarize this important connection below.

Joint and Conditional Precision.
The precision matrix of the conditional distribution xA∣xBx_A|x_BxA​∣xB​ is given by PA∣B=(CA∣B)−1=PA.(9) P_{A|B} = (C_{A|B})^{-1} = P_A. \tag{9} PA∣B​=(CA∣B​)−1=PA​.(9) In words, the conditional precision is obtained by deleting the rows and columns of the joint precision PPP that involve the conditioning variables xBx_BxB​.

This connection also leads us to our main result, which states that conditional independence can be inferred from zero entries of the precision matrix. This result follows immediately by rearranging (9) to CA∣B=(PA)−1(10) C_{A|B} = (P_A)^{-1} \tag{10} CA∣B​=(PA​)−1(10) and noting that (PA)−1(P_A)^{-1}(PA​)−1 is simply a two-by-two matrix that we can consider inverting by hand.

Zeros in Precision imply Conditional Independence.
An entry PijP_{ij}Pij​, i≠ji \neq ji​=j of the joint precision matrix is zero if and only if xix_ixi​ and xjx_jxj​ are conditionally independent, given all other variables. That is, Pij=0  ⟺  Cov[xi,xj∣xB]=0  ⟺  xi⊥xj∣xB,(11) P_{ij} = 0 \iff \text{Cov}[x_i,x_j|x_B] = 0 \iff x_i \perp x_j | x_B, \tag{11} Pij​=0⟺Cov[xi​,xj​∣xB​]=0⟺xi​⊥xj​∣xB​,(11) where B:={1,…,n}∖{i,j}B := \{1, \dots, n\} \setminus \{i,j\}B:={1,…,n}∖{i,j}.

Proof. Setting A:={i,j}A := \{i,j\}A:={i,j}, the above derivation showed CA∣B=(PA)−1C_{A|B} = (P_A)^{-1}CA∣B​=(PA​)−1, where PAP_APA​ is the two-by-two block of the joint precision PPP corresponding to the variables xix_ixi​ and xjx_jxj​. Thus, Cov[xi,xj∣xB]=[CA∣B]12=0  ⟺  [(PA)−1]12=0. \text{Cov}[x_i,x_j|x_B] = [C_{A|B}]_{12} = 0 \iff [(P_A)^{-1}]_{12} = 0. Cov[xi​,xj​∣xB​]=[CA∣B​]12​=0⟺[(PA​)−1]12​=0. We use the well-known formula for the inverse of a two-by-two matrix to obtain \begin{align} (P_A)^{-1} &= \begin{bmatrix} P_{ii} & P_{ij} \newline P_{ji} & P_{jj} \end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{P_{ii}P_{jj}- P_{ij}^2} \begin{bmatrix} P_{jj} & -P_{ji} \newline -P_{ij} & P_{ii}\end{bmatrix}. \end{align} Notice that the off-diagonal entries of PAP_APA​ and (PA)−1(P_A)^{-1}(PA​)−1 are the same, up to a minus sign. This means Cov[xi,xj∣xB]=[CA∣B]12=0  ⟺  Pij=0, \text{Cov}[x_i,x_j|x_B] = [C_{A|B}]_{12} = 0 \iff P_{ij} = 0, Cov[xi​,xj​∣xB​]=[CA∣B​]12​=0⟺Pij​=0, so the result is proved. The fact that conditional uncorrelatedness implies conditional independence follows from the fact that xA∣xBx_A|x_BxA​∣xB​ is Gaussian. ■\qquad \blacksquare■

The above result interprets the zero values of off-diagonal elements of the precision matrix. Later in this post we will revisit the precision, and see that non-zero values can be interpreted through the lens of partial correlation. An interpretation of the magnitude of the diagonal elements is more straightforward, and is given in the below result.

Diagonal Entries of Precision.
The diagonal entry PiiP_{ii}Pii​ of the precision gives the reciprocal of the variance of xix_ixi​, conditional on all other variables; that is, Pii=Var[xi∣x∼i]−1(12) P_{ii} = \text{Var}[x_i|x_{\sim i}]^{-1} \tag{12} Pii​=Var[xi​∣x∼i​]−1(12)

Proof. We know from (9) that CA∣B=(PA)−1C_{A|B} = (P_A)^{-1}CA∣B​=(PA​)−1. Let A:={i}A := \{i\}A:={i} and B:={1,…,n}∖{i}B := \{1, \dots, n\} \setminus \{i\}B:={1,…,n}∖{i}. It follows that Var[xi∣x∼i]=CA∣B=(PA)−1=Pii−1.■ \text{Var}[x_i|x_{\sim i}] = C_{A|B} = (P_A)^{-1} = P_{ii}^{-1}. \qquad \qquad \blacksquare Var[xi​∣x∼i​]=CA∣B​=(PA​)−1=Pii−1​.■

Notions of Linear Conditional Dependence

In this section, we introduce two analogs of the correlation coefficient that quantify the linear dependence between two variables, conditional on another set of variables. We show that if all random variables are jointly Gaussian, then the two notions coincide.

Conditional Correlation

We start by defining conditional correlation, which is nothing more than the typical notion of correlation but defined with respect to a conditional probability measure. We give the definition with respect to our current setup, but of course the notion can be generalized.

Definition: conditional correlation.
For a pair of indices {i,j}\{i,j\}{i,j} and its complement B:={1,…,n}∖{i,j}B := \{1, \dots, n\} \setminus \{i,j\}B:={1,…,n}∖{i,j}, we define the conditional covariance between xix_ixi​ and xjx_jxj​, given xBx_BxB​, as Cov[xi,xj∣xB]:=EB[(xi−EB[xi])(xj−EB[xj])],(13) \text{Cov}[x_i,x_j|x_B] := \mathbb{E}^B\left[(x_i-\mathbb{E}^B[x_i])(x_j-\mathbb{E}^B[x_j]) \right], \tag{13} Cov[xi​,xj​∣xB​]:=EB[(xi​−EB[xi​])(xj​−EB[xj​])],(13) where we denote EB[⋅]:=E[⋅∣xB]\mathbb{E}^B[\cdot] := \mathbb{E}[\cdot|x_B]EB[⋅]:=E[⋅∣xB​]. The conditional correlation is then defined as usual by normalizing the covariance (13): Cor[xi,xj∣xB]:=Cov[xi,xj∣xB]Var[xi∣xB]Var[xj∣xB].(14) \text{Cor}[x_i,x_j|x_B] := \frac{\text{Cov}[x_i,x_j|x_B]}{\sqrt{\text{Var}[x_i|x_B]\text{Var}[x_j|x_B]}}. \tag{14} Cor[xi​,xj​∣xB​]:=Var[xi​∣xB​]Var[xj​∣xB​]​Cov[xi​,xj​∣xB​]​.(14)

The conditional correlation is simply a correlation where all expectations involved are conditional on xBx_BxB​. It is sometimes also denoted as ρij∣B:=Cor[xi,xj∣xB]\rho_{ij|B} := \text{Cor}[x_i,x_j|x_B]ρij∣B​:=Cor[xi​,xj​∣xB​].

Partial correlation

We now consider an alternative notion of conditional linear dependence that is defined with respect to underlying linear regression models. For generality, we provide a definition that quantifies dependence between sets of variables xA1x_{A_1}xA1​​ and xA2x_{A_2}xA2​​, after removing the confounding effect of a third set xBx_{B}xB​. However, our primary interest will be in the special case A1={i}A_1 = \{i\}A1​={i} and A2={j}A_2 = \{j\}A2​={j}, which aligns with the conditional correlation definition given above.

Definition: partial correlation.
For a set of random variables x1,…,xnx_1, \dots, x_nx1​,…,xn​, let A1,A2⊂{1,…,n}A_1,A_2 \subset \{1, \dots, n\}A1​,A2​⊂{1,…,n} be index sets and BBB a third index set disjoint from the other two. Define the linear regression coefficients \begin{align} \alpha_{A_1}^\star, \beta_{A_1}^\star &:= \text{argmin}_{\alpha,\beta} \mathbb{E}\lVert x_{A_1} - \alpha + \beta_{A_1}^\top x_B)\rVert^2 \tag{15} \newline \alpha_{A_2}^\star, \beta_{A_2}^\star &:= \text{argmin}_{\alpha,\beta} \mathbb{E} \lVert x_{A_2} - (\alpha + \beta_{A_2}^\top x_B)\rVert^2, \end{align} and associated residuals \begin{align} e_{A_1} &:= x_{A_1} - (\alpha_{A_1}^\star + [\beta_{A_1}^\star)^\top x_B] \tag{16} \newline e_{A_2} &:= x_{A_2} - (\alpha_{A_2}^\star + [\beta_{A_2}^\star)^\top x_B]. \end{align} The partial correlation coefficient between xA1x_{A_1}xA1​​ and xA2x_{A_2}xA2​​ given xBx_BxB​ is defined as ρA1A2⋅B:=Cor[eA1,eA2].(17) \rho_{A_{1}A_{2} \cdot B} := \text{Cor}[e_{A_1}, e_{A_2}]. \tag{17} ρA1​A2​⋅B​:=Cor[eA1​​,eA2​​].(17)

The intuition here is that the residuals from the regressions contain variation that is unexplained by xBx_BxB​, so that ρA1A2⋅B\rho_{A_{1}A_{2} \cdot B}ρA1​A2​⋅B​ quantifies the linear dependence between xA1x_{A_1}xA1​​ and xA2x_{A_2}xA2​​ after removing the effect of xBx_BxB​. We emphasize the importance of the word “linear” here, as linearity plays a role in two different ways in the above definition. Recall that the typical correlation coefficient measures linear dependence, so the statement in (17) is a measure of linear dependence between the residuals. Moreover, the residuals themselves are defined via linear regressions, and hence the sense in which the effect of xBx_BxB​ is “removed” also relies on linearity assumptions. Note also that we are allowing xA1x_{A_1}xA1​​ and xA2x_{A_2}xA2​​ to be sets of variables, so the linear regressions considered above are multi-output regressions, which can essentially be thought of as a set of independent univariate regressions, since the loss function simply sums the error across the outputs. Thus, in general, the α\alphaαs and β\betaβs are vectors and matrices, respectively.

Equivalence for Gaussian Distributions

We now introduce the additional assumption that x1,…xnx_1, \dots x_nx1​,…xn​ are jointly Gaussian and show that in this setting the definitions of conditional and partial correlation are equivalent. For more detailed discussion on these connections, see (Baba et al., 2004) and (Lawrance, 1976).

Conditional and Partial Correlation for Gaussians.
Suppose that x1,…,xnx_1, \dots, x_nx1​,…,xn​ are jointly Gaussian, with positive definite covariance CCC. For a pair of indices {i,j}\{i,j\}{i,j} and its complement B:={1,…,n}∖{i,j}B := \{1, \dots, n\} \setminus \{i,j\}B:={1,…,n}∖{i,j}, we have ρij⋅B=Cor[xi,xj∣xB].(18) \rho_{ij \cdot B} = \text{Cor}[x_i,x_j|x_B]. \tag{18} ρij⋅B​=Cor[xi​,xj​∣xB​].(18) That is, under joint Gaussianity the notions of conditional and partial correlation coincide.

Proof. The result will be proved by establishing that Cov[ei,ej]=Cov[xi,xj∣xB](19) \text{Cov}[e_i,e_j] = \text{Cov}[x_i,x_j|x_B] \tag{19} Cov[ei​,ej​]=Cov[xi​,xj​∣xB​](19) for any iii and jjj (possibly equal), where eie_iei​ and eje_jej​ are the residual random variables defined in (16) with A1:={i}A_1 := \{i\}A1​:={i} and A2:={j}A_2 := \{j\}A2​:={j}. The i=ji=ji=j case establishes the equality of the variances, meaning that the correlations will also be equal. To this end, we start by noting that the righthand side in (19) is given by the relevant entry of the matrix CA∣B=CA−CABCB−1CBA. C_{A|B} = C_A - C_{AB}C_B^{-1}C_{BA}. CA∣B​=CA​−CAB​CB−1​CBA​. where A:={i,j}A := \{i,j\}A:={i,j}. Recall that CA∣BC_{A|B}CA∣B​ is defined in (4). By extracting the relevant entry of CA∣BC_{A|B}CA∣B​ we have Cov[xi,xj∣xB]=Cij−CiBCB−1CBj.(20) \text{Cov}[x_i,x_j|x_B] = C_{ij} - C_{iB}C_B^{-1}C_{Bj}. \tag{20} Cov[xi​,xj​∣xB​]=Cij​−CiB​CB−1​CBj​.(20) We now show that Cov[ei,ej]\text{Cov}[e_i,e_j]Cov[ei​,ej​] reduces to (20). We recall that the conditional expectation for a square-integrable random variable is given by the projection E[xi∣xB]=argming∥xi−g(xB)∥2,(21) \mathbb{E}[x_i|x_B] = \text{argmin}_{g} \lVert x_i - g(x_B)\rVert^2, \tag{21} E[xi​∣xB​]=argming​∥xi​−g(xB​)∥2,(21) where the minimum is considered over all xBx_BxB​-measurable functions ggg. In our present Gaussian setting, this is solved by E[xi∣xB]=E[xi]+⟨ki,xB−E[xB]⟩,(22) \mathbb{E}[x_i|x_B] = \mathbb{E}[x_i] + \langle k_i, x_B-\mathbb{E}[x_B]\rangle, \tag{22} E[xi​∣xB​]=E[xi​]+⟨ki​,xB​−E[xB​]⟩,(22) where ki=Cov[xB]−1Cov[xB,xi]=CB−1CiBk_i = \text{Cov}[x_B]^{-1} \text{Cov}[x_B,x_i] = C_B^{-1}C_{iB}ki​=Cov[xB​]−1Cov[xB​,xi​]=CB−1​CiB​. See this post for the derivation of (22). The important thing to notice is that (22) is a linear function of xBx_BxB​, which means that it solves the linear regression problem (15); i.e., αA1⋆+(βA1⋆)⊤xB=E[xi∣xB]\alpha^{\star}_{A_1} + (\beta^{\star}_{A_1})^\top x_B = \mathbb{E}[x_i|x_B]αA1​⋆​+(βA1​⋆​)⊤xB​=E[xi​∣xB​]. Thus, ei=xi−E[xi∣xB]=xi−E[xi]−⟨ki,xB−E[xB]⟩.(23) e_i = x_i - \mathbb{E}[x_i|x_B] = x_i - \mathbb{E}[x_i] - \langle k_i, x_B-\mathbb{E}[x_B]\rangle. \tag{23} ei​=xi​−E[xi​∣xB​]=xi​−E[xi​]−⟨ki​,xB​−E[xB​]⟩.(23) and similarly for eje_jej​. Notice that the constants E[xi]\mathbb{E}[x_i]E[xi​] and E[xB]\mathbb{E}[x_B]E[xB​] will be dropped when taking covariances, so it suffices to treat these as zero. We thus have \begin{align} \text{Cov}[e_i,e_j] &= \text{Cov}[x_i-\langle k_i, x_B\rangle,x_j-\langle k_j, x_B\rangle] \newline &= C_{ij} + k_i^\top C_B k_j - C_{iB}k_j - k_i^\top k_j - k_i^\top C_{Bj} \newline &= C_{ij} + C_{iB}C_B^{-1}C_B C_B^{-1}C_{Bj} - 2C_{iB}C_B^{-1}C_{Bj} \newline &= C_{ij} - C_{iB}C_B^{-1}C_{Bj}. \end{align} We see that the final expression agrees with (20), so the result is proved. ■\qquad \blacksquare■

Precision Matrix and Partial Correlation

Having defined partial and conditional correlation, we now return to the question of interpreting the off-diagonal elements of a Gaussian precision matrix. Throughout this section we assume that x1,…,xnx_1, \dots, x_nx1​,…,xn​ are jointly Gaussian with positive definite covariance CCC and precision matrix PPP. The following definition normalizes the precision matrix, analogous to the way a covariance matrix is normalized to produce a correlation matrix.

Normalized Precision.
Define the normalized precision Pˉ\bar{P}Pˉ as the matrix with elements Pˉij:=PijPiiPjj.(24) \bar{P}_{ij} := \frac{P_{ij}}{\sqrt{P_{ii}P_{jj}}}. \tag{24} Pˉij​:=Pii​Pjj​​Pij​​.(24)

We are now ready to establish the connection between the (normalized) precision and the notions of conditional and partial correlation. Note that the diagonal elements of Pˉ\bar{P}Pˉ satisfy Pˉii=1\bar{P}_{ii}=1Pˉii​=1. The following result interprets the off-diagonal elements.

Off-Diagonal Entries of Precision.
Assume that x1,…,xnx_1, \dots, x_nx1​,…,xn​ are jointly Gaussian with normalized precision matrix Pˉ\bar{P}Pˉ. Let {i,j}\{i,j\}{i,j} be a pair of distinct indices and B:={1,…,n}∖{i,j}B := \{1,\dots,n\} \setminus \{i,j\}B:={1,…,n}∖{i,j} its complement. Then Pˉij=−ρij⋅B=−Cor[xi,xj∣xB].(25) \bar{P}_{ij} = -\rho_{ij\cdot B} = -\text{Cor}[x_i,x_j|x_B]. \tag{25} Pˉij​=−ρij⋅B​=−Cor[xi​,xj​∣xB​].(25) In words, the off-diagonal entry Pˉij\bar{P}_{ij}Pˉij​ is equal to the negated partial correlation between xix_ixi​ and xjx_jxj​ given all other variables. It is likewise equal to the negated conditional correlation.

Proof. Let A:={i,j}A := \{i,j\}A:={i,j}. Recall from (9) that \begin{align} C_{A|B} &= (P_A)^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} P_{ii} & P_{ij} \newline P_{ji} & P_{jj} \end{bmatrix}^{-1} = \gamma \begin{bmatrix} P_{jj} & -P_{ij} \newline -P_{ji} & P_{ii} \end{bmatrix}, \tag{26} \end{align} with γ:=(PiiPjj−Pij2)−1\gamma := (P_{ii}P_{jj}-P^2_{ij})^{-1}γ:=(Pii​Pjj​−Pij2​)−1. We have again used the expression for the inverse of a two-by-two matrix. The equality in (26) gives \begin{align} \text{Var}[x_i|x_B] &= \gamma P_{jj} \newline \text{Var}[x_j|x_B] &= \gamma P_{ii} \newline \text{Cov}[x_i,x_j|x_B] &= -\gamma P_{ij}. \end{align} We thus have Pˉij=PijPiiPjj=−Cov[xi,xj∣xB]Var[xi∣xB]Var[xj∣xB]=−Cor[xi,xj∣xB]. \bar{P}_{ij} = \frac{P_{ij}}{\sqrt{P_{ii}P_{jj}}} = -\frac{\text{Cov}[x_i,x_j|x_B]}{\sqrt{\text{Var}[x_i|x_B]\text{Var}[x_j|x_B]}} = -\text{Cor}[x_i,x_j|x_B]. Pˉij​=Pii​Pjj​​Pij​​=−Var[xi​∣xB​]Var[xj​∣xB​]​Cov[xi​,xj​∣xB​]​=−Cor[xi​,xj​∣xB​]. This establishes the relationship between the precision elements and conditional correlation. Under the Gaussian assumption, the equivalence with the partial correlation follows from (18). ■\qquad \blacksquare■
