Markov Melding

Combining and splitting models in a Bayesian framework.

Published

03 November 2024

In this post, I summarize a technique called Markov melding, originally proposed in Goudie et al (2023).

Motivation and Setup

Models of complex systems (climate, disease, etc.) are typically composed of many sub-models. For example, climate models couple together models of the atmosphere, land, ocean, and cryosphere. The component models may be developed largely in isolation, before being combined with the others to form a complex model for the whole system. In the context of Markov melding, we are considering that these component models are specified within a Bayesian framework, so that each component can be identified with a joint probability distribution of the component model parameters and data. Letting MMM denote the number of components, we denote the density for the joint distribution of the mthm^{\text{th}}mth submodel by \begin{align} &p_m(\phi, \psi_m, Y_m), &&m = 1, \dots, M \tag{1} \end{align} where YmY_mYm​ denotes the data used in the mthm^{\text{th}}mth submodel. The submodel parameters are θm:=(ϕ,ψm),(2) \theta_m := (\phi, \psi_m), \tag{2} θm​:=(ϕ,ψm​),(2) and we make a distinction between component-specific parameters ψm\psi_mψm​ and parameters ϕ\phiϕ that are shared across all submodels. Goudie et al (2019) refer to ϕ\phiϕ as the link parameters. A typical modeling workflow might involve obtaining the posterior distributions pm(ϕ,ψm∣Ym)p_m(\phi, \psi_m|Y_m)pm​(ϕ,ψm​∣Ym​) for each submodel independently, and then combining them post-hoc in some fashion to study the system as a whole. Bayesian melding is an alternative approach that seeks to construct a joint distribution p(ϕ,ψ1,…,ψm,Y1,…,Ym)p(\phi, \psi_1, \dots, \psi_m, Y_1, \dots, Y_m)p(ϕ,ψ1​,…,ψm​,Y1​,…,Ym​) that combines all components into a unified probabilistic model. Then inference can proceed by characterizing the posterior distribution p(ϕ,ψ1,…,ψm∣Y1,…,Ym)p(\phi, \psi_1, \dots, \psi_m | Y_1, \dots, Y_m)p(ϕ,ψ1​,…,ψm​∣Y1​,…,Ym​). The key distinction here is that all of the data Y1,…,YmY_1, \dots, Y_mY1​,…,Ym​ is used to inform the parameters across all components. The isolated component posterior pm(ϕ,ψm∣Ym)p_m(\phi, \psi_m|Y_m)pm​(ϕ,ψm​∣Ym​) might look quite different from p(ϕ,ψm∣Y1,…,Ym)p(\phi, \psi_m|Y_1, \dots, Y_m)p(ϕ,ψm​∣Y1​,…,Ym​) due to the influence of the other components.

On a final note before proceeding with the method, we emphasize the assumption that ϕ\phiϕ is the only link between the submodels. From a probabilistic standpoint, this implies that we will be considering joint distributions with the property that component-specific variables in any two distinct models are conditionally independent given the link parameter ϕ\phiϕ; symbolically, (ψm,Ym)⊥(ψℓ,Yℓ)∣ϕ,m≠ℓ.(3) (\psi_m, Y_m) \perp (\psi_\ell, Y_\ell) | \phi, \qquad m \neq \ell. \tag{3} (ψm​,Ym​)⊥(ψℓ​,Yℓ​)∣ϕ,m​=ℓ.(3)

Markov Combination

We start with the assumption that the submodels are consistent in the sense that they all imply the same prior distribution on the link parameter ϕ\phiϕ; i.e., p1(ϕ)=⋯=pM(ϕ).(4) p_1(\phi) = \cdots = p_M(\phi). \tag{4} p1​(ϕ)=⋯=pM​(ϕ).(4) The method of constructing a joint distribution under assumption (4) is referred to as Markov combination (Dawid and Lauritzen, 1993). This assumption makes the definition of a joint distribution relatively straightforward. Denoting the common prior on the link parameter by p(ϕ):=pm(ϕ)p(\phi) := p_m(\phi)p(ϕ):=pm​(ϕ), the Markov combination approach defines a joint distribution pcombp_{\text{comb}}pcomb​ by \begin{align} p_{\text{comb}}(\phi, \psi_1, \dots, \psi_M, Y_1, \dots, Y_M) &:= p_{\text{comb}}(\phi, \psi_1, \dots, \psi_M, Y_1, \dots, Y_M|\phi)p(\phi) \newline &:= p(\phi) \prod_{m=1}^{M} p_m(\psi_m, Y_m|\phi). \tag{5} \end{align} We emphasize the both of the above equalities are definitions, though both definitions are quite natural in this setting. The first ensures that the common prior p(ϕ)p(\phi)p(ϕ) is preserved by the joint distribution, while the second defines the conditional density (conditional on ϕ\phiϕ) using the conditional independence properties noted in (3). This ensures the submodel distributions pm(ϕ,ψm,Ym)p_m(\phi, \psi_m, Y_m)pm​(ϕ,ψm​,Ym​) are also preserved. Specifically, (5) satisfies pcomb(ϕ,ψm,Ym)=pm(ϕ,ψm,Ym),m=1,…,M.(6) p_{\text{comb}}(\phi, \psi_m, Y_m) = p_m(\phi, \psi_m, Y_m), \qquad m = 1, \dots, M. \tag{6} pcomb​(ϕ,ψm​,Ym​)=pm​(ϕ,ψm​,Ym​),m=1,…,M.(6) It will be useful to rewrite (5) as \begin{align} p_{\text{comb}}(\phi, \psi_1, \dots, \psi_M, Y_1, \dots, Y_M) &= p(\phi) \prod_{m=1}^{M} p_m(\psi_m, Y_m|\phi) \newline &= p(\phi) \prod_{m=1}^{M} \frac{p_m(\phi, \psi_m, Y_m)}{p(\phi)} \newline &= \frac{1}{p(\phi)^{M-1}} \prod_{m=1}^{M} p_m(\phi, \psi_m, Y_m), \tag{7} \end{align} which shows that pcombp_{\text{comb}}pcomb​ is just the product of the submodel distributions pmp_mpm​, normalized by p(ϕ)M−1p(\phi)^{M-1}p(ϕ)M−1 to account for the fact that the prior p(ϕ)p(\phi)p(ϕ) is multiplied MMM times in the product. From the expression (7), we may easily verify (6); letting ψ−m:=(ψ1,…,ψm−1,ψm+1,…,ψM)\psi_{-m} := (\psi_1, \dots, \psi_{m-1}, \psi_{m+1}, \dots, \psi_M)ψ−m​:=(ψ1​,…,ψm−1​,ψm+1​,…,ψM​) and similarly for Y−mY_{-m}Y−m​, we obtain \begin{align} p_{\text{comb}}(\phi, \psi_m, Y_m) &= \int p_{\text{comb}}(\phi, \psi_1, \dots, \psi_M, Y_1, \dots, Y_M) d\psi_{-m}dY_{-m} \newline &= \frac{1}{p(\phi)^{M-1}}p_m(\phi, \psi_m, Y_m) \prod_{\ell \neq m} \int p_m(\phi, \psi_m, Y_m) d\psi_\ell dY_\ell \newline &= \frac{1}{p(\phi)^{M-1}}p_m(\phi, \psi_m, Y_m) \prod_{\ell \neq m} p_\ell(\phi) \newline &= \frac{1}{p(\phi)^{M-1}}p_m(\phi, \psi_m, Y_m) p(\phi)^{M-1} \newline &= p_m(\phi, \psi_m, Y_m). \end{align}

While the definition (5) seems somewhat natural, one may wish for a more quantitative justification. To this end, Massa and Lauritzen (2010) show that pcombp_{\text{comb}}pcomb​ has maximal entropy among all distributions satisfying the MMM constraints (6). Loosely speaking, this tells us that pcombp_{\text{comb}}pcomb​ is the least constrained of all the distributions satisfying (6), a desirable property in the absence of additional knowledge on the relative merits of the submodels.

Markov Melding

Goudie et al (2019) consider propose a generalization of Markov combination to the setting where the marginals pm(ϕ)p_m(\phi)pm​(ϕ) may not be consistent across submodels. In this case, we can no longer simply pull out the common prior in the first line of definition (5). To address this, the authors’ propose first replacing the marginals pm(ϕ)p_m(\phi)pm​(ϕ) with a common “pooled” prior ppool(ϕ)p_{\text{pool}}(\phi)ppool​(ϕ). After performing this “marginal replacement”, Markov combination can then be applied with respect to the modified submodels.

Marginal Replacement

We first consider modifying the submodels to ensure they are consistent with respect to the marginal of the link parameter. The idea is to replace the original submodels pm(ϕ,ψm,Ym)=pm(ϕ)pm(ψm,Ym∣ϕ) p_m(\phi, \psi_m, Y_m) = p_m(\phi) p_m(\psi_m, Y_m | \phi) pm​(ϕ,ψm​,Ym​)=pm​(ϕ)pm​(ψm​,Ym​∣ϕ) with modifications that share a common ϕ\phiϕ-marginal: \begin{align} p_{\text{repl},m}(\phi, \psi_m, Y_m) &:= p_{\text{pool}}(\phi) p_m(\psi_m, Y_m | \phi) \tag{8} \newline &= \frac{p_{\text{pool}}(\phi)}{p_m(\phi)} p_m(\phi, \psi_m, Y_m). \tag{9} \end{align} We see in (8) that the submodel conditional (on ϕ\phiϕ) is unchanged by the marginal replacement. The expression (9) gives an alternative viewpoint, where we view the original joint submodel distribution pmp_mpm​ as re-weighted by some modification factor ppool(ϕ)/pm(ϕ)p_{\text{pool}}(\phi)/p_m(\phi)ppool​(ϕ)/pm​(ϕ). This, of course, begs the question of how to choose ppool(ϕ)p_{\text{pool}}(\phi)ppool​(ϕ). Naturally, we would hope this distribution adequately summarizes the original marginals p1(ϕ),…,pM(ϕ)p_1(\phi), \dots, p_M(\phi)p1​(ϕ),…,pM​(ϕ) in some sense. We put aside this question for the time being, and assume that we have fixed some pooled prior ppool(ϕ)p_{\text{pool}}(\phi)ppool​(ϕ). Given that a common prior ppool(ϕ)p_{\text{pool}}(\phi)ppool​(ϕ) has been chosen, Goudie et al (2019) provides some justification for the definition (9): prepl,m(ϕ,ψm,Ym)=argminq∈QDKL(q∣∣pm), p_{\text{repl},m}(\phi, \psi_m, Y_m) = \text{argmin}_{q \in \mathcal{Q}} \text{D}_{\text{KL}}(q || p_m), prepl,m​(ϕ,ψm​,Ym​)=argminq∈Q​DKL​(q∣∣pm​), where Q={q(ϕ,ψm,Ym)∣q(ϕ)=ppool(ϕ) ∀ϕ}. \mathcal{Q} = \left\{q(\phi, \psi_m, Y_m) | q(\phi) = p_{\text{pool}}(\phi) \ \forall \phi \right\}. Q={q(ϕ,ψm​,Ym​)∣q(ϕ)=ppool​(ϕ) ∀ϕ}. In words, the marginally replaced distribution minimizes the KL-divergence with respect to pmp_mpm​ over all distributions with ϕ\phiϕ-marginals equal to ppool(ϕ)p_{\text{pool}}(\phi)ppool​(ϕ).

Markov Combination with respect to the modified submodels

We can now apply Markov combination with respect to the modified submodels prepl,1,…,prepl,Mp_{\text{repl},1}, \dots, p_{\text{repl},M}prepl,1​,…,prepl,M​. Following (5), define \begin{align} p_{\text{meld}}(\phi, \psi_1, \dots, \psi_M, Y_1, \dots, Y_M) &:= p_{\text{pool}}(\phi) \prod_{m=1}^{M} p_{\text{repl},m}(\psi_m, Y_m|\phi) \newline &= p_{\text{pool}}(\phi) \prod_{m=1}^{M} p_{m}(\psi_m, Y_m|\phi), \tag{10} \end{align} where the second equality uses the fact that marginal replacement preserves the conditionals (see (9)). We can alternatively expand (10) as pmeld(ϕ,ψ1,…,ψM,Y1,…,YM)=ppool(ϕ)∏m=1Mpm(ϕ,ψm,Ym)pm(ϕ),(11) p_{\text{meld}}(\phi, \psi_1, \dots, \psi_M, Y_1, \dots, Y_M) = p_{\text{pool}}(\phi) \prod_{m=1}^{M} \frac{p_{m}(\phi, \psi_m, Y_m)}{p_m(\phi)}, \tag{11} pmeld​(ϕ,ψ1​,…,ψM​,Y1​,…,YM​)=ppool​(ϕ)m=1∏M​pm​(ϕ)pm​(ϕ,ψm​,Ym​)​,(11) which provides a direct comparison to (7). All of the facts noted for Markov combination still apply here, but now with respect to the marginally replaced models prepl,mp_{\text{repl},m}prepl,m​, not the original submodels. In particular, we have that pmeld(ϕ,ψm,Ym)=prepl,m(ϕ,ψm,Ym),m=1,…,M,(12) p_{\text{meld}}(\phi, \psi_m, Y_m) = p_{\text{repl},m}(\phi, \psi_m, Y_m), \qquad m = 1, \dots, M, \tag{12} pmeld​(ϕ,ψm​,Ym​)=prepl,m​(ϕ,ψm​,Ym​),m=1,…,M,(12) but in general prepl,m(ϕ,ψm,Ym)≠pm(ϕ,ψm,Ym),(13) p_{\text{repl},m}(\phi, \psi_m, Y_m) \neq p_{m}(\phi, \psi_m, Y_m), \tag{13} prepl,m​(ϕ,ψm​,Ym​)​=pm​(ϕ,ψm​,Ym​),(13) meaning that p(meld)p(\text{meld})p(meld) does not preserve the submodel joint distributions. Inference

Metropolis-within-Gibbs

The structure of (22) suggests updating the component-specific parameters ψm\psi_mψm​ one at a time, and hence a Metropolis-within-Gibbs (MwG) scheme.
